2. long-form literary text

Moon Palace review from the thoughts page. The primary use case for a body text font.

Bitter

For all my dissatisfaction with how Auster writes several of the female characters in Moon Palace (especially Kitty Wu and Mrs Hume), I still cannot resist the novel's startling literariness. This is one of those books where Auster's machinery of chance, drift, and sudden revelation finally acquires real grace. The plot keeps turning almost by accident, as if the novel were discovering its own destiny in passing, and one keeps reading in a kind of daze, half for the story and half for the pleasure of how casually it is told. Even people who find Auster schematic elsewhere often admit that Moon Palace is where his coincidences feel truly like gorgeously egregious myth.

What I love most is the way the book lets deprivation, wandering, and dependency exist without sinking into self-pity. Marco Fogg and his uncle's life together is precarious, but never miserable in the usual literary way. The same goes for Fogg's homelessness in Central Park, which Auster writes with a strange blend of material exactness and metaphysical lightness. He is already doing what he does best here: turning New York into both a real city and a philosophical apparatus, a place where lines like 'A here exists only in relation to a there' can appear almost offhandedly and still reorganise the whole novel.

5. second review — denser literary criticism

The Crying of Lot 49 review. More <em> usage, longer sentences, higher register.

Bitter

Pynchon's sentences in The Crying of Lot 49 go off like little grenades. The instant one reads them, they are already in pieces, and the only option is to crouch amongst the debris with Oedipa Maas and start assembling a world from shards. That is not a flaw. It is the whole pleasure of his books. Oedipa fights through, like the reader, the shards of 1960s Southern California, trying to solve the Tristero mystery, or else persuade herself that no mystery exists at all. In front of her, every clue opens not onto resolution, but onto another layer of noise, pattern, and possible hallucination.

All of that gives this debut novel of Pynchon's so much of its potency. Beneath all the jokes, acronyms, stamps, muted horns, and communication breakdowns, Lot 49 is about a woman standing opposite Leviathan and finding that the alternative network may be no less maddening than the official one. Oedipa is part Josef K., part noir detective, part doomed initiate. She wants revelation, but what she gets is the modern world in Pynchon's preferred form: overconnected, underexplained, and forever humming just below the threshold of proof.
