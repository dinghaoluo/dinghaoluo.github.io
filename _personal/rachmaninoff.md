Sergei Vasilyevich Rachmaninoff (Сергей Васильевич Рахманинов) was born on 1 April 1873 (New Style; 20 March in the Julian calendar) near Veliky Novgorod in north-western Russia, in a small town called Semyonovo (Семёново), though the family soon moved to their residence in Onega (Онега). Both parents were amateur pianists, and the young Rachmaninoff took naturally to the instrument: from the age of four, his mother, Butakova (Бутакова), gave him unbroken piano lessons. Although his early progress was unremarkable, his grandfather Arkady Rachmaninoff (Аркадий Рахманинов) was determined to see him become a professional pianist, and brought in Anna Ornatskaya (Анна Орнацкая) from St Petersburg (then the capital of the Russian Empire) as his new teacher.

- 1880, age 7: first composition, *Romance in A minor, Op. Posth.*, for violin and piano.

Rachmaninoff's father, Vasily Rachmaninoff (Василий Рахманинов), had enjoyed a distinguished early career, serving in the Imperial Army before becoming a locally respected pianist, but the second half of his life was consumed by gambling. This single vice wagered away the family's fortunes; they were forced to sell the Onega residence and move to a small flat in St Petersburg. Unsurprisingly, Rachmaninoff never forgave the episode. In old age he still described his father as 'a compulsive gambler and a skirt chaser' [1].

Shortly after the move, in 1883, the ten-year-old Rachmaninoff enrolled at the St Petersburg Conservatory, arranged by Ornatskaya, herself a graduate and current teacher there. That same year, his sister Sofia (Софиа) died of diphtheria, and his father abandoned the family, relocating to Moscow. Two years later, his other sister, Yelena (Елена), died of pernicious anaemia. The blow was compounded by the fact that it was Yelena who had introduced him to the music of Tchaikovsky (Пётр Чайковский), with whom Rachmaninoff would later share a brief acquaintance. Perhaps in response to these losses, Rachmaninoff began to resist his studies. In the spring examinations of 1885 he 'successfully' failed his general subjects. Ornatskaya had no choice but to inform his mother that the boy might not be able to progress.

His mother then consulted her nephew, Alexander Siloti (Александр Зилоти). Siloti was among the Russian Empire's more prominent pianists, a student of Franz Liszt, and on good terms with Tchaikovsky. He advised that Rachmaninoff be sent to the Moscow Conservatory to study under his own former teacher, Nikolai Zverev (Николай Зверев): Moscow offered a better musical atmosphere, and Zverev's stricter pedagogy, Siloti believed, would better serve the young pianist's development.

- 1886, age 13: *Étude in F-sharp major*, for piano.
- 1887, age 14: *Lento in D minor*, for piano.

Two years after enrolment, in 1887, Rachmaninoff won his first scholarship, the Rubinstein Scholarship (named after the pianist and composer Anton Rubinstein). That same year he graduated from the junior division and advanced to the senior level, studying piano under Siloti.

- 1887, age 14: *Scherzo in D minor*, for orchestra. Dedicated to Siloti.

In the summer of 1890, Rachmaninoff arrived at Ivanovka (Ивановка), an estate near Tambov. Until 1917, his summers would frequently include a period of composition there. The surrounding countryside proved a lasting source of inspiration. The following July, barely three months past his eighteenth birthday, he completed his Opus 1: *Piano Concerto No. 1 in F-sharp minor*, dedicated to Siloti. This was not his first attempt at a piano concerto; as early as 1889 he had drafted a simpler one in C minor (a key that would shortly become his most famous signature). Rachmaninoff was deeply attached to the First Concerto. Years later, in a letter to his friend Albert Swan, he wrote: 'My First Concerto... it is full of the beauty of youth. But nobody cares. I told the American audience I would play my First Concerto; although nobody objected, I could see from their faces that they wanted the Second or the Third. They did not care about the First' [2].

That December he composed his first symphonic poem, *Prince Rostislav* (Князь Ростислав), after Tolstoy's poem of the same name. Like many of his early works, it was never performed in his lifetime, published posthumously in 1947.

Siloti left the Moscow Conservatory at the end of the year. Fearing the Conservatory would assign him an uncongenial teacher, Rachmaninoff resolved to bring forward his graduation piano examination from 1892 to 1891. Even Siloti lacked confidence in this plan, but Rachmaninoff passed his piano, composition and theory examinations in 1891 and received the Conservatory's honorary degree. In February 1892 he completed his *Trio élégiaque No. 1 in G minor* (for piano, violin and cello). In March he performed his First Concerto at the Moscow Conservatory for the first time. In April he wrote the one-act opera *Aleko* (Алеко) in seventeen days; it premiered in May at the Bolshoi Theatre. Tchaikovsky attended. Predictably, this was the first time Rachmaninoff received Tchaikovsky's praise. *Aleko* won Rachmaninoff the Conservatory's highest student award, the Great Gold Medal. Even his once-strict teacher Zverev personally presented him with his own gold pocket watch in recognition. Having graduated with the title of 'Free Artist', Rachmaninoff tasted his first genuine success.

- 1892, age 19: *Two Pieces, Op. 2*, for cello and piano.
- 1892, age 19: *Cinq Morceaux de Fantaisie, Op. 3*, for piano. This set includes one of his most famous pieces, the *Prelude in C-sharp minor*, which demonstrates his renowned command of chordal writing (and his extraordinary hand span): the opening three massive chords alone give a taste.

From graduation onward, the 'Free Artist' earned a monthly salary of fifteen roubles teaching piano. In September 1892 he appeared at the Moscow Electrical Exhibition, where he premiered the now-iconic *Prelude in C-sharp minor* (a piece that would become a source of enduring exasperation, since every subsequent audience demanded it).

In the summer of 1893, Rachmaninoff completed *The Rock* (Утёс, Op. 7) and had the honour of performing the fantasy before Tchaikovsky. Tchaikovsky admired the work and invited Rachmaninoff to perform it during his European tour the following year [3]. Rachmaninoff accepted.

In November of that year, before the tour, Tchaikovsky died.

- 1893, age 20: *Six Songs, Op. 4*, for voice and piano.
- 1893, age 20: *Suite No. 1, Op. 5*, for two pianos. Dedicated to Tchaikovsky (composed while he was still alive; premiered after his death).
- 1893, age 20: *Deux Morceaux de Salon, Op. 6*, for violin and piano.
- 1893, age 20: *Six Songs, Op. 8*.
- 1893, age 20: *Trio élégiaque No. 2, Op. 9*, for piano, violin and cello (expressing his anguish at Tchaikovsky's sudden death).
- 1894, age 21: *Sept Morceaux de Salon, Op. 10*, for piano.
- 1894, age 21: *Six Morceaux, Op. 11*, for piano.
- 1894, age 21: *Capriccio Bohémien, Op. 12*, for orchestra.

Tchaikovsky's sudden death left an irreparable mark. Through 1894 and 1895 the grief was audible in everything Rachmaninoff composed. For a long period he lost all inspiration and returned to teaching piano for a living. By the end of 1895, pressed for money, he was forced to join a three-month national tour with the Italian violinist Teresina Tua, but the venture went so badly that he withdrew midway, forfeiting all fees. In desperation, he pawned the gold pocket watch that Zverev had given him.

Fortunately, by September 1895 he had already completed his *Symphony No. 1 in D minor, Op. 13*. Unfortunately, it was this work that would push him into depression. The premiere was scheduled for March 1897, and in the two intervening years, burdened by work, he did not once hear the symphony performed.

- 1896, age 23: *Twelve Songs, Op. 14*.
- 1896, age 23: *Six Choruses for Women's or Children's Voices, Op. 15* (written for an Orthodox church's Sunday service, for money).
- 1896, age 23: *Six Moments Musicaux, Op. 16*, for piano.

On 28 March 1897, the premiere of the First Symphony took place in St Petersburg, conducted by Alexander Glazunov (Александр Глазунов). Glazunov squandered rehearsal time and was not an especially gifted conductor. Contemporary musicians described the performance simply as a 'disaster'; Rachmaninoff left the hall in fury before it ended. Also present was his future wife, Natalya Satina (Наталиа Сатина), who wrote in her memoirs: '...Glazunov was absolutely drunk while conducting...' (Glazunov's most famous pupil, Shostakovich, even recalled in his own memoirs that Glazunov kept a tube running from a concealed bottle behind his desk and would sip from it whenever he was not speaking). Of course, the behaviour may simply have reflected contempt for Rachmaninoff: years later, in 1930, Rachmaninoff presented Glazunov with a manuscript of his Fourth Concerto, which was subsequently found abandoned on the back seat of Glazunov's taxi by the driver.

Whatever the cause of Glazunov's failure, Rachmaninoff's reaction was bleak. In his diary he wrote: 'My First Symphony gave me no pleasure whatsoever; it brought me only bitterness and sorrow...' [4]. He never performed the work again in his lifetime. The second public performance came in 1945, two years after his death, after the war was over.

For three years following 1897, Rachmaninoff was caught in the vortex of depression, composing almost nothing, occasionally serving as assistant conductor at the Moscow Russian Opera Company. During this period Satina became engaged to him, but the engagement was immediately opposed by the Orthodox Church and her family. Fittingly, the only completed work of those years was a piece called *Fate* (Судьба). (He did also produce scattered oddities such as *Were You Hiccuping?*, but nothing seriously composed.)

Then came Nikolai Dahl (Николай Даль).

From January to April 1900, Rachmaninoff underwent a programme of daily hypnosis and psychotherapy devised by Dahl, aimed at stabilising his sleep, appetite and creative state. After four months, he wrote in his diary that he felt '...new inspiration beginning to flow again' [5]. The following year, having completed the *Suite No. 2, Op. 17*, Rachmaninoff composed his most famous work: *Piano Concerto No. 2 in C minor, Op. 18*, dedicated to Dahl. Written in the aftermath of depression, it is suffused with a quiet sorrow that nonetheless harbours a thread of hope, mirroring his state of mind.

The Second Concerto's first movement (1901) was composed after the second and third (1900). It opens with a pianissimo subdominant chord in F, and closes with a fortissimo dominant chord in C minor, as though narrating the aimless searching and struggle of depression. After bar eleven, the first theme is introduced by arpeggiated piano figures circling the theme while the cello states it. At bar thirty-four the violins enter, bridging the first section into the second. In bars fifty-five to sixty-three the piano finally regains the lead, entering an *un poco più mosso* passage that concludes the first theme.

The second theme begins at bar seventy-five, introduced by woodwinds, brass and upper strings, driving towards a fortissimo chord in D-flat major at bar seventy-nine, whereupon everything halts abruptly, awaiting the piano's re-entry. The piano melody of the second theme is crucial: it reaches its climax in the second bar, followed by six bars of suppression and lingering. After multiple reprises, the piano again, as with the first theme, concludes in an *un poco più mosso* passage from bar 141.

The remainder of the first movement restates these two themes across five further sections, from quiet to powerful and back again. At last, after the climax at the end of the tenth section fades, the piano re-enters at the *Meno mosso* opening of the eleventh section with the arpeggios from the first section, introducing the coda. In the coda's second part, the piano enters a *poco a poco accelerando* passage and concludes the first movement in three fortissimo dominant chords. The second movement then transposes the C minor of the first movement's close into E major (via arpeggios resembling those of the opening), and E major is in turn restored to C minor in the third movement, which closes in C major.

The complete premiere took place on 9 November 1901, conducted by Siloti, with Rachmaninoff as soloist. It was a great success, earning him his first Glinka Prize.

- 1901, age 28: *Cello Sonata in G minor, Op. 19*, for piano and cello.

On 12 May 1902, after a three-year engagement, Rachmaninoff married Satina, his cousin. They moved to Moscow and had two daughters.

- 1902, age 29: *Spring Cantata, Op. 20*.
- 1902, age 29: *12 Songs, Op. 21*.

In 1903 he completed his most ambitious project to date, the *Variations on a Theme of Chopin, Op. 22*. The following year, illness among his daughters nearly halted his output. By the end of 1905 he had taken a six-month post as chief conductor at the Bolshoi Theatre. The First Russian Revolution, though it ultimately failed, severely disrupted the country's cultural life; Rachmaninoff lost interest in conducting. In February 1906 he resigned and took his wife and daughters briefly to Italy. When they fell ill in March, he brought them back to Ivanovka to convalesce. Without employment, he could sustain himself only by composing [6].

- 1903, age 30: *Ten Preludes, Op. 23*, for piano.
- 1905, age 32: *The Miserly Knight, Op. 24* (opera).
- 1905, age 32: *Francesca da Rimini, Op. 25* (opera). Both operas premiered at the Bolshoi under his baton.

In November 1906, with Russia in political turmoil, Rachmaninoff moved his family to Dresden. The years there rekindled his inspiration: on a chance visit to a gallery in Leipzig, he encountered Arnold Böcklin's painting *Die Toteninsel* (The Isle of the Dead), which he 'adapted' into a symphonic poem in 1908 (*Isle of the Dead, Op. 29*, in A minor).

He had begun his *Symphony No. 2* (Op. 27) in 1906 and premiered it in February 1908. The reception exceeded his expectations and earned him his second Glinka Prize, restoring his creative confidence.

- 1906, age 33: *Fifteen Songs, Op. 26*.
- 1908, age 35: *Piano Sonata No. 1 in D minor, Op. 28* (inspired by Goethe's *Faust*; part of his 'Dresden Trilogy' with the Second Symphony and the unfinished opera *Monna Vanna*. The premiere received a lukewarm response, and the sonata remained among his least-performed works).

The following year, at Ivanovka, he completed his *Piano Concerto No. 3 in D minor, Op. 30*, then departed for an American tour. Notably, the concerto's second performance was conducted by Gustav Mahler. Though the tour was extremely well received, homesickness drove Rachmaninoff to return to Russia almost immediately, in early February 1910.

He was then appointed Vice-President of the Imperial Russian Musical Society, and completed the *Liturgy of St John Chrysostom, Op. 31* by year's end. In recognition of his achievements, he was also named lifelong conductor of the Moscow Philharmonic Society (though in 1912 he resigned in protest upon learning that the Society had dismissed a musician on grounds of Jewish heritage).

- 1910, age 37: *Thirteen Preludes, Op. 32*, for piano.
- 1911, age 38: *Études-Tableaux, Op. 33*.
- 1912, age 39: *Fourteen Songs, Op. 34*.

At the end of 1912, having resigned, Rachmaninoff received an anonymous letter from Russia at his home in Switzerland, enclosing a poem by Edgar Allan Poe: 'The Bells'. Deeply inspired, he composed the celebrated work of the same name (*Op. 35*) by year's end. After completing his *Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor, Op. 36*, his daughters fell ill with typhoid, forcing him to interrupt his work and take them to Berlin for treatment.

Although his daughters had largely recovered by the end of 1914, fate was not finished: war broke out on 28 July 1914. The following year, his friend Taneyev (Сергей Танеев) and his father died in quick succession. Overwhelmed by grief, Rachmaninoff completed one of his most sorrowful works, *Vocalise*, at the end of 1915, later included in his *Twelve Romances* as an appendix to the 1913 *Fourteen Songs*.

What he did not foresee was that another upheaval was imminent.

- 1915, age 42: *All-Night Vigil, Op. 37*.
- 1916, age 43: *Six Songs, Op. 38*.
- 1916, age 43: *Études-Tableaux, Op. 39*, for piano.

In February 1917 (Julian calendar), the February Revolution began in St Petersburg. Within five days the Duma assumed power, established a provisional government, and the Russian Empire became the Russian Republic. Lenin, in opposition, arrived in the renamed 'Petrograd' in April to call upon the peoples of Europe to join the proletarian revolution, overthrow the provisional government, and revolt anew.

Rachmaninoff, standing with the working people, performed 'revolutionary concerts' in Petrograd and Yalta. He did not know that the Yalta performance would be his last on Russian soil. At the end of the year the October Revolution began; his estate at Ivanovka was seized by the Bolsheviks. In his anxiety, Rachmaninoff received an invitation to tour Scandinavia and accepted without hesitation, using it as pretext to flee with his family.

The turmoil once again severed his compositional impulse. From late 1917 he was in constant transit, filling his free time as soloist with various orchestras, perpetually learning and rehearsing new repertoire, with little time to compose. In November 1918 he received invitations from the Boston and Cincinnati Symphony Orchestras to serve as conductor. He declined, but after careful deliberation decided to move his family to America. Their first address was 505 West End Avenue, Manhattan. Shortly after settling, Steinway presented him with a piano for practice and composition. At 505, the Rachmaninoff household continued to live a Russian life: Russian food, Russian servants, Russian customs.

Post-war America was peaceful, and Rachmaninoff had ample time to recover. Although his next major original work would not appear until 1926, he spent the intervening years arranging famous pieces by other composers, including the well-known *Liebesleid* by Fritz Kreisler.

In 1926 Rachmaninoff composed his final concerto: *Piano Concerto No. 4 in G minor, Op. 40*, dedicated to Nikolai Medtner (Николай Метнер), a close friend who reciprocated by dedicating his own Second Concerto to Rachmaninoff. The two shared many compositional affinities, such as a fondness for virtuoso technique and instrumental interplay, though they diverged in areas like the treatment of arpeggios and chords. The 'first version' of the Fourth Concerto (1926) was not well received, prompting substantial revisions and a 'second version' in 1928; this too failed to excite audiences. Rachmaninoff withdrew the score, reworked it, and published a 'third version' in 1941, which is the one most commonly performed today.

- 1927, age 54: *Three Russian Songs, Op. 41*.
- 1931, age 58: *Variations on a Theme of Corelli, Op. 42*, for piano.

It was during these years that Rachmaninoff befriended Vladimir Horowitz (Владимир Горовиц). The two supported and encouraged each other throughout their creative lives; Rachmaninoff publicly praised Horowitz's interpretation of the Third Concerto [7].

The years of Russian turmoil had sealed the thought of return inside his head. After 1921 he never attempted to go back to his homeland, developing instead a particular emotional complex, the gossamer threads of exile intertwined with sorrow, the 'burden of sorrow' that defined his late years. From 1931, the fifty-eight-year-old Rachmaninoff faced growing demand for tours: audiences in the shadow of the Depression and on the eve of the Second World War wanted to hear his music, mournful yet laced with threads of hope. Though he was no longer financially pressed, the relentless touring schedule left him exhausted and unable to compose. Between 1931 and his illness in 1942, he produced only four completed works: the *Variations on a Theme of Corelli*; the celebrated *Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini, Op. 43*; *Symphony No. 3 in A minor, Op. 44*; and *Symphonic Dances, Op. 45*. During this period he also released his complete recorded piano works under the Victor Talking Machine Company (later RCA Victor), with whom he had signed years earlier.

Who could have imagined: before the war had ended, Rachmaninoff was already gravely ill. In early 1942 he moved with his wife and daughters to a villa in Beverly Hills, California. He was suffering from arteriosclerosis, lower back pain, neuralgia and hypertension. After completing his final recording session, he purchased and moved into 610 Elm Drive. On 1 February 1943, Rachmaninoff and his family became American citizens. Shortly afterwards he was diagnosed with melanoma.

And so he prepared his own funeral. His final performance took place on 17 February at the University of Tennessee. He played Chopin's *Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor, Op. 35*. The sonata's third movement is also known as the *Marche funèbre* [8].

On 26 March he lost consciousness. On the 28th, Rachmaninoff died at his home in Beverly Hills.

He missed his seventieth birthday by four days. His remains are buried at Kensico Cemetery, Westchester County, New York. The tombstone is inscribed only in a language he never mastered, English. Of Russian there is no trace.

Rachmaninoff never returned to the homeland he longed for.
