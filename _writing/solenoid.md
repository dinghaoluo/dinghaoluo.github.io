<span class="dropcap">L</span>ice came before the fourth dimension in Cărtărescu's ***Solenoid***. The novel opens at the protagonist's scalp, where metaphysics touches skin. Before the buried solenoids and levitating bodies, there is our protagonist, a schoolteacher, over a sink, combing lice from his hair and noticing, with horrible calm, the shared organic indignity of their bodies and his. Revelation grows from itch; cosmic escape begins as infestation. The surreal and the metaphysical stem from the real and physical.

The book makes its first metaphysical claim in this humiliating register: the scalp, the belly button, the tooth, the classroom, the tramline, the blind wall of a neighbour's house. *Surrealism* is too clean a word for this. It assumes a stable reality later breached by dream. *Solenoid* begins from a harsher suspicion: the normal reality *is the same thing* as the strange dream. It's just that in childhood, strangeness is the first condition of reality; adulthood teaches us which parts of it to call normal.

Cărtărescu lays this bare in black and white in *Solenoid*: 'Nothing is strange to a child, because he lives in the strange.' The sentence gives this piece its title because it describes the book more precisely than any genre label. Childhood, in Cărtărescu's formulation, has little to do with innocence, nostalgia, or a decorative reservoir of wonder. It is a cognitive condition before the world has been sorted into permissible and impermissible categories; before dream, memory, shame, illness, hallucination, and fact have been assigned to separate drawers. The adult mind, schooled by language, fear, work, embarrassment, and the ordinary police force of other people, later calls that sorting sanity. Cărtărescu writes from before the drawers close.

That is why the loose category of surrealism only gets one so far. It still thinks in terms of breach: reality here, dream there, impossibility arriving as a foreign body. *Solenoid* begins from a more disturbing premise. Irina floating above the bed is not a violation of the room so much as one of the room's hidden laws becoming briefly visible; the solenoids beneath Bucharest do not make the city strange so much as expose the fact that its ordinary surfaces were never sufficient. The impossible events arrive less as intrusions than as belated evidence that the world was never settled.

> In childhood, strangeness is the first condition of reality;<br>
> adulthood teaches us which parts of it to call normal.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact .pullquote--wide}

My own first approach to the book repeated the mistake the opening diagnoses. Before reading *Solenoid*, I had known Cărtărescu mostly as a Nobel-rumour name, which is to say I knew him as a category rather than a writer. I expected a large Eastern European novel whose ambitions could be named before its sentences were felt: late-communist Bucharest, failed literary vocation, childhood, memory, metaphysics pressing through ordinary life. That description is not wrong. It is worse than wrong: it is correct in the way a low-resolution image is correct, with all the pressure removed. What matters is the texture that breaks the category open: the lice and navel, the school corridors, Colentina Hospital, the soap and textile factories on the tram route, Irina pressing the wall button that lifts the bed, the Picketists outside the morgue, the Voynich manuscript passing through the archive like a dream-object with paperwork.

The Pynchon comparison matters because it marks the point where maximalism changes direction. Cărtărescu himself has said, in an [International Booker interview](https://thebookerprizes.com/the-booker-library/features/mircea-cartarescu-and-sean-cotter-interview-solenoid), that *V.* (1963) was the book that impressed him most when he first began writing literature. The affinity is obvious: both writers have the maximalist nerve to treat the novel as a total form, where joke, archive, dream, machine, body, political ruin, and mystical system all belong to the same circuitry. But scale is the easy similarity. Direction is the useful difference. Pynchon pushes outward into military infrastructure, corporations, cartels, rockets, information systems, technologies of domination. Cărtărescu pushes inward, into the phenomenology of being trapped inside a body and a city one cannot quite verify as real. Pynchon gives paranoia its comic physics: everything connects, and connection itself becomes terrifying. Cărtărescu's terror is insufficiency: reality connects, but not enough; it answers, but in the wrong language.

That difference clarifies the whole book. Cărtărescu's world does not feel false. It feels too thin for the pressure inside it, as though another version of existence were pressing against this one, separated from us by skin, language, architecture, and some missing word we were never taught to pronounce. The rest of the novel gives that absence geography.

Bucharest is where that pressure first becomes spatial. Cărtărescu has said, in a [2022 interview](https://theuntranslated.wordpress.com/2022/10/06/mircea-cartarescus-interview-for-de-reactor-on-solenoid-mites-butterflies-kafka-untranslatability-censorship-a-paradise-in-ruins-and-his-latest-novel-theodoros/), that he recreated and invented Bucharest, making it an alter ego, a twin, a state of mind. For once, the authorial gloss locates the pressure without shrinking it. The book's Bucharest is not realistic city plus supernatural disturbance. It is tramlines, factories, hospitals, schools, apartment blocks, vacant lots, libraries, and childhood routes already distorted by attention. The marvels do not decorate this city. They reveal how much violence and dream were already stored in its ordinary coordinates.

That is why the remembered city keeps becoming anatomical. The boat-shaped house, the hidden solenoids, Irina levitating above the bed, Palamar's library and its later revelation of mites, the school that expands into nightmarish corridors: each scene feels like a local exposure of the city's tissue. When the narrator follows tram 21 to the edge of Bucharest, or moves through school corridors that feel secreted rather than built, setting turns into organ. The city's topography becomes indistinguishable from the topography of thought because Cărtărescu has made thought topographical in the first place: routed, blocked, infected, electrified, full of chambers one did not know one had entered.

Magical realism feels too placid as a frame for this. It leaves the critic politely counting layers: reality below, magic above, coexistence announced as if coexistence explained anything. *Solenoid* dissolves the layering. Memory behaves like an unreliable imaging technique; the narrator behaves like both subject and lesion. The city is not simply perceived by a damaged consciousness, nor is consciousness merely projected onto the city. The two systems keep passing through each other until the boundary between perception and world becomes diagnostically useless.

Once the city and mind are entangled, knowledge itself starts to lose its clean edges. Reading *Solenoid*, one repeatedly loses confidence in the boundary between Cărtărescu's inventions and the historical record. Ethel Lilian Voynich first enters through *The Gadfly*, then the name opens into Wilfrid Voynich and the unreadable manuscript. Charles Howard Hinton brings the fourth dimension with him. Occult genealogies of science and literature begin to look less like research than like dream finding supporting documents. Pynchon performs a related operation, filling fiction with realia until fact itself seems conspiratorial. In *Solenoid*, the facts do stranger, quieter damage. They feel like objects that have passed through dream and returned with their surfaces changed.

The same doubling reaches biography, which is why the narrator's failed poem matters more than plot summary makes it sound. In the novel, *The Fall* is humiliated at the Workshop of the Moon and the narrator becomes a schoolteacher. In [Federico Perelmuter's account](https://astra-mag.com/articles/this-invented-nation/), the fictional *The Fall* shadows Cărtărescu's *The Levant*: the real workshop reception was rapturous where the narrator's is ruinous. This is not a clever autobiographical key. It is the book's metaphysics at the scale of a life. A doubled city finds its narrator in a doubled biography; the book is built from the branch that did not happen, or perhaps from the life that literary institutions refused to certify.

The cheap version would make failure a romance, with rejection purifying the artist and success made vulgar by comparison. Cărtărescu refuses that consolation. Failure makes his narrator porous: open to resentment, revelation, tenderness, grandiosity, self-pity, disgust, and forms of attention that a cleaner life might have sealed off. This is where the novel became uncomfortably personal for me. The humiliation at the Workshop of the Moon is not merely a bad literary evening. It is public misclassification: a room deciding what kind of person the narrator is allowed to become. I distrust institutional vindication even when I want it, perhaps especially when I want it; *Solenoid* understands how quickly an accident hardens into a biography. Its counterfactual offers no revenge of the misunderstood genius. It makes both success and failure harder to aestheticise.

The archive might make the novel sound like a system of signs. The body prevents that escape. In a *Chicago Review* interview, Cărtărescu explicitly invokes [*soma sema*](https://www.chicagoreview.org/soma-sema-an-interview-with-mircea-cartarescu/), the body as prison. My materialist reflex resists the clean separation. Mind happens as body, through history, environment, appetite, injury, and other people. Yet the phenomenology of imprisonment is real. One can reject soul-body dualism and still feel the insult of embodiment: a capacity for infinity tied to skin, sweat, spinal pain, teeth, infection, appetite, decay.

Here the body-horror becomes structural. The body gives the metaphysical problem its evidence and its insult. Lice, mites, sweat, navels, dental shame, bodily fluids, illness, the obscene smallness of organisms: Cărtărescu keeps dragging transcendence back through pores. The fourth dimension matters because the third dimension has teeth, skin, infection. The desire to escape existence is generated by existence at its most intimate.

But *Solenoid* does not let escape remain private. If disgust asks how one might flee the body, the house-on-fire dilemma asks what escape is worth when another body remains inside the burning room. Cărtărescu describes the decisive choice as one between saving a masterpiece and saving a child; the narrator chooses the child. In the same interview, Cărtărescu links that choice to solidarity, to the refusal to save only oneself. This is why the Picketists matter. Their protest against death is absurd because death cannot negotiate, yet moving because protest is the only political grammar available for metaphysical injustice. The enemy is mortality itself; the form is still collective: bodies gathering, shouting, keeping vigil outside cemeteries and the Mina Minovici morgue. No one exits alone without turning salvation into another cruelty.

By this point, labels have become bad instruments. They point without measuring. Childhood, Bucharest, failure, archive, body, salvation: each name works for a moment, then becomes too narrow. Hooyman's [labyrinth](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/a-romanian-daedalus-surrealist-labyrinth-on-mircea-cartarescus-solenoid/) remains the right spatial metaphor for bewilderment. The title's object adds the missing physics.

The solenoid under the house matters first as an object: the thing itself, the hum beneath the ordinary, the force that warps the floorboards and lifts a woman from her bed. The device also gives the novel its syntax. A solenoid is a coil that produces a magnetic field when current passes through it; Cărtărescu builds narrative current the same way, by making recurrence generate force. At first the returns seem almost formal: episode coils around episode, dream around memory, the fictional Bucharest around the historical one. Then the loops begin to carry ethical and conceptual friction. Failed life coils around successful life; science around mysticism; disgust around tenderness; the longing to leave the body around the child who must be saved inside it. The movement is induction rather than ordinary progression. Repeated scenes accumulate charge before they disclose a system.

That principle explains why summary fails. The book's true unit is the charged scene: the poetry humiliation, the classrooms, the endless corridors, Colentina and the factories along the tramline, the impossible domestic machinery of the house, Palamar's mites under the lens, the Picketists outside the morgue, the sense that Bucharest has always had an underside waiting for the right pressure. Even the most outlandish images retain a practical texture: a button pressed, a route followed, a body placed under observation, a manuscript card found in a library catalogue. These scenes do not assemble into an argument in a linear way. They alter the reader's conductivity. The novel trains one's categories to fail, then makes that failure feel like a form of perception.

> Repeated scenes accumulate charge<br>
> before they disclose a system.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact .pullquote--wide}

If the book's meanings keep pressing against available language, translation becomes one of its metaphysical tests. Sean Cotter's English is inseparable from that pressure. I do not read Romanian; my Cărtărescu is necessarily Cotter's Cărtărescu in English, and the dependence matters. I cannot judge fidelity from outside the language. I can judge whether the English has pulse, density, and pressure. A novel like this could die in translation in several ways: all ornament, no pulse; all archive, no fever; all strangeness smoothed into prestige-literary mist. Cotter's English keeps the sentences dense without making them airless. It also preserves inquiry, the sense that every object has to be touched, worried, opened, suspected.

I feel this partly as a translator. The title sentence has to sound almost childishly plain while carrying a metaphysical insult; much of the book asks for that doubleness. The English keeps scholarship and hallucination in friction. Translation belongs to the metaphysics itself because the whole book is obsessed with missing words, unreadable manuscripts, and meanings that press against available language without fully entering it.

By the end, the book had altered my tolerance for incompleteness. The Voynich manuscript, the fourth dimension, the hidden solenoids, the failed poem, the city's underside, the body's prison, the missing word in the riddle of the world: all of them circle the same absence. The question that gives the whole book its gravitational pull is brutally simple: how does one get saved? *Solenoid* never answers by filling the absence. It withholds the word and makes the withholding physical.

*Solenoid* is one of the great reading experiences of my life not because it solved that question, but because it made the question bodily. Pynchon remains the writer who first taught me to love fictional totality; Cărtărescu has made that territory less solitary. Across nearly seven hundred pages, the book restored an embarrassing suspicion: press hard enough on reality's walls and something might answer from the other side.
