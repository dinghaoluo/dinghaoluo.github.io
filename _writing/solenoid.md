<p><span class="dropcap">S</span><em>olenoid</em> makes the adult category of the surreal feel provincial. A surreal event, ordinarily understood, is a violation: the real world proceeds according to its sober rules, and then something impossible intrudes. Cărtărescu refuses that arrangement. In his book, the impossible does not break into reality from outside. It behaves like something reality had been suppressing, a pressure already present inside rooms, bodies, childhood memories, school corridors, tramlines, abandoned lots, libraries, skin.</p>

That is what the line that gives this piece its title names for me: 'Nothing is strange to a child, because he lives in the strange.' It describes the book's method more precisely than any genre label. Childhood, in *Solenoid*, has little to do with innocence, nostalgia, or a decorative reservoir of wonder. It is a cognitive condition before the world has been sorted into permissible and impermissible categories. Dreams, old memories, shame, illness, hallucination, fact: all of them arrive in the same material. The adult mind later organises them into separate drawers and calls the result sanity. Cărtărescu writes from before that sorting has fully succeeded.

Before reading the novel I knew Cărtărescu mostly as a Nobel-rumour name, which is to say I knew a category rather than a writer. I expected a large European novel of recognisable ambition: late-communist Bucharest, failed literary vocation, childhood, memory, a slow inflation of the ordinary into metaphysical significance. Some of that description is technically correct, yet it misses the actual violence of the book. *Solenoid* does not inflate ordinary life. It makes ordinary life look like a bad reduction of experience, a low-resolution export from a stranger original.

The English-language reception has understandably circled a small constellation of terms. Ben Hooyman, writing in the *Los Angeles Review of Books*, makes the [labyrinth](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/a-romanian-daedalus-surrealist-labyrinth-on-mircea-cartarescus-solenoid/) central. Federico Perelmuter, in *Astra*, reads the novel as a mutation of [autofiction, history fiction, and science fiction](https://astra-mag.com/articles/this-invented-nation/). Nicholas Dames, in *n+1*, calls it an [antinovel](https://www.nplusonemag.com/issue-45/reviews/outside-the-museum-of-literature-1/) and begins, tellingly, from lice. The *New Yorker* notice places the book near [Kafka](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2022/11/14/diaghilevs-empire-feral-city-when-we-were-sisters-and-solenoid), while James Webster at *Full Stop* reaches for [Penrose stairs and impossible objects](https://www.full-stop.net/2022/12/21/reviews/james-webster/solenoid-mircea-cartarescu/) as analogues for its structure. These readings are useful because each names a real pressure: spatial bewilderment, counterfactual autobiography, hostility to literature as a museum of tasteful effects, the body's humiliating insistence. Yet the book's force for me lies in the way those pressures fuse. The labyrinth is not only spatial. The antinovel is not only formal. The body-horror is not only grotesque. The impossible object is not merely a formal puzzle. All of them become instruments for asking why reality feels both excessive and insufficient.

That is where my reading parts slightly from the usual architecture of comparison. Kafka supplies the bug, the failed audience, the human being waking into a category error. Pynchon supplies the paranoid grid, the sense that a whole civilisation can be read as a machine for converting persons into signals. *Solenoid* absorbs both, but its strongest move is to make the child's relation to reality prior to both Kafka's trial and Pynchon's system. The world is not first rational and then broken. It is first strange, and adulthood is the local political regime that teaches us which parts of the strangeness to call normal.

The coordinate I reached for was Pynchon. Cărtărescu himself has said, in [an International Booker interview](https://thebookerprizes.com/the-booker-library/features/mircea-cartarescu-and-sean-cotter-interview-solenoid), that *V.* was the book that impressed him most when he first began writing literature, and the affinity is real. Both writers have the maximalist nerve to treat the novel as a total form: joke, archive, dream, machine, body, political ruin, mystical system, all passing through the same circuitry. The direction of travel differs. Pynchon pushes outward into military infrastructure, corporations, cartels, rockets, information systems, technologies of domination. Cărtărescu pushes inward, into the phenomenology of being trapped inside a body and a city one cannot quite verify as real.

The difference matters because calling *Solenoid* a Romanian Pynchon feels both tempting and wrong. Pynchon gives paranoia its great comic physics: everything connects, and connection itself becomes terrifying. Cărtărescu gives metaphysical homesickness a physical geography.

  <p>The world is not overconnected<br>so much as under-redeemed.</p>
</blockquote>

One feels that another version of existence is pressing against this one, separated from us by skin, language, architecture, and some missing word we were never taught to pronounce.

Bucharest is the membrane through which that pressure passes. Cărtărescu has said, in [a 2022 interview](https://theuntranslated.wordpress.com/2022/10/06/mircea-cartarescus-interview-for-de-reactor-on-solenoid-mites-butterflies-kafka-untranslatability-censorship-a-paradise-in-ruins-and-his-latest-novel-theodoros/), that the Bucharest of his books is an invented double of the real city, an alter ego, a state of mind. This is the rare authorial explanation that clarifies without shrinking the work. The book's Bucharest has no stable realistic substrate underneath the marvels. It is a remembered city, which means it is already distorted by attention, fear, repetition, childhood scale, bodily discomfort, and the obscure emotional weather of place. The ship-shaped house, the hidden solenoids, Irina descending from the ceiling, Palamar's library of mites, the school that expands into nightmare: these do not feel like fantasy intrusions. They feel like the city's organs becoming briefly visible.

I find that more convincing than magical realism as a frame. Magical realism often implies two layers, reality and magic, with the critic's task reduced to describing their coexistence. *Solenoid* is stranger because it dissolves the layering. Bucharest behaves like an organism with buried machinery; the fantastic functions as anatomy rather than garnish. When the narrator follows tramlines to the city's edge, or moves through school corridors that feel less built than secreted, the city seems to be showing not its symbolic meaning but its tissue structure. Memory behaves like an unreliable imaging technique; the narrator behaves like both subject and lesion. The novel keeps asking what a life looks like when the boundary between perception and world has become diagnostically useless.

That question gives the failed-writer premise its force. In the novel, the narrator's poem is humiliated and he becomes a schoolteacher. In life, Cărtărescu's corresponding poetic debut was successful. Perelmuter is right to treat this inversion as more than biographical trivia: the book is built from the life that did not happen, or perhaps from the life that literature's institutions refused to certify. This could easily have produced a familiar myth of failure, in which rejection ennobles the artist and success appears vulgar by comparison. Cărtărescu avoids that cheap romance. His narrator does not become purer because he fails. He becomes more porous: open to resentment, revelation, tenderness, grandiosity, self-pity, disgust, and forms of attention that a cleaner life might have sealed off.

This is the point at which the novel became uncomfortably personal for me. The scene of humiliation is not merely an origin story; it is a public misclassification, a room deciding what kind of person the narrator is allowed to become. I distrust institutional vindication even when I want it, and perhaps especially when I want it. Schools, prizes, publications, fellowships, famous names: they matter materially, sometimes brutally, but they also turn accident into biography with frightening efficiency. Once the institution has approved a version of one's life, contingency begins to look like destiny. *Solenoid* stages the obscene inverse. The unapproved life, the botched life, the branch that should have been pruned, becomes the only branch capable of producing the vision. That is not consoling. It makes both success and failure harder to aestheticise.

The same uncertainty governs the book's relation to knowledge. Reading *Solenoid*, one repeatedly loses confidence in the boundary between Cărtărescu's inventions and the historical archive. The Voynich manuscript appears; for a second, absurdly, it feels as though he must have invented that too. Then Charles Hinton appears, then *The Gadfly*, then fourth-dimensional geometry, then occult genealogies of science and literature, and the archive begins to feel oneiric without becoming fake. Pynchon performs a related operation, filling fiction with realia until fact itself seems conspiratorial. Cărtărescu's effect is less paranoid. His facts feel like objects that have passed through dream and returned with their surfaces changed.

My neuroscience-trained suspicion of the book becomes useful here rather than obstructive. *Solenoid* is full of Gnostic pressure. In [a 2025 interview with *Chicago Review*](https://www.chicagoreview.org/soma-sema-an-interview-with-mircea-cartarescu/), Cărtărescu explicitly invokes *soma sema*, the body as prison. Dames's review is especially sharp on this point because it refuses to treat the body as a mere metaphor; the fascination with flesh is not a decorative grotesque layer but the book's philosophical engine. Scientifically, I do not believe in that clean separation. Minds are not luminous exiles trapped inside meat; they are processes of bodies, histories, environments, and other bodies. Yet the phenomenology of imprisonment is real. One can know, abstractly, that mind and body are not separate substances, and still feel the insult of embodiment: a capacity for infinity tied to skin, sweat, spinal pain, teeth, infection, appetite, decay.

The contradiction is exactly why the book's body-horror matters. Its metaphysics never escapes tissue. Lice, mites, sweat, skin, navels, dental shame, bodily fluids, illness, the obscene smallness of organisms: Cărtărescu keeps dragging transcendence back through pores. The narrator's yearning for the fourth dimension would be far less powerful if it floated free of the body's humiliations. Instead, the desire to escape existence is generated by existence at its most intimate.

> The body produces the metaphysical problem.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact}

That is also what keeps the book from becoming an abstract anti-world manifesto. The narrator wants escape, yet the novel slowly forces escape to pass through ethics. Cărtărescu has described the decisive dilemma as the choice between saving the masterpiece and saving the child from a burning house. The narrator chooses the child. That choice matters because it interrupts the fantasy of private salvation. If the body is a prison, it is at least a prison shared with children, lovers, students, the sick, the ridiculous Picketists in their mortuary of protest, the dead whose traces continue to structure the living. Their picketing is absurd on the surface, yet the absurdity clarifies the point: even metaphysical revolt has to borrow the forms of collective protest, placards and gatherings and bodies standing together in a room. No one exits alone without turning salvation into another cruelty.

The book's form enacts this pressure better than a clean plot could. Hooyman's labyrinth is the right starting image, but I think the title's object gives an even sharper formal metaphor. A solenoid is a coil that produces a magnetic field when current passes through it, and Cărtărescu's structure works by a similar principle. Episode coils around episode; dream coils around memory; the fictional Bucharest coils around the historical one; failed life coils around successful life; science coils around mysticism; disgust coils around tenderness. The movement is induction rather than ordinary progression. After enough repetitions and returns, distinctions that seemed stable begin to tilt.

Summary fails for the same reason. The book's true unit is the charged scene rather than the plot point: the poetry humiliation, the classrooms, the endless corridors, the factory-like hospital, the tram reaching the edge of the city, the impossible domestic machinery of the house, Palamar's mites under the lens, the sense that Bucharest has always had an underside waiting for the right pressure. Even the most outlandish images retain a practical texture: a button pressed, a ceiling opening, a route followed, a body placed under observation. These scenes do not assemble into an argument in a linear way. They alter the reader's conductivity. By the time the larger metaphysical machinery becomes visible, it feels less like an explanation than an induced current.

Sean Cotter's translation is inseparable from that effect. I do not read Romanian, so my Cărtărescu is necessarily Cotter's Cărtărescu in English. That dependence should be stated plainly. A dead translation would have made this book unbearable: all ornament, no pulse. Cotter keeps the sentences dense without making them airless. His most important achievement is the preservation of inquiry. Reference management clearly matters in a book like this; still, the English prose keeps the narrator's pressure of asking, the sense that every object has to be touched, worried, opened, suspected.

I feel this partly as a translator. The hardest sentences are often the ones whose strangeness has to remain strange without becoming vague. Explain too much and the sentence dies; beautify too much and it lies. *Solenoid* in English repeatedly walks that line. Scholarship and hallucination remain in friction. Precision and fever occupy the same sentence without cancelling each other. For a novel so invested in missing words and unreadable manuscripts, that is not a secondary achievement. It is part of the metaphysics.

By the end, the book had altered my tolerance for incompleteness. The Voynich manuscript, the fourth dimension, the hidden solenoids, the failed poem, the city's underside, the body's prison, the missing word in the riddle of the world: all of them circle the same absence. *Solenoid* does not solve that absence, and its greatness depends on the refusal. It makes the missing word palpable.

For me, then, *Solenoid* became, almost against my own caution, one of the great reading experiences of my life. It is surreal, ingenious, learned, grotesque, tender, and large, though those are still the less interesting terms. Across nearly seven hundred pages, it restores the embarrassing suspicion that literature can test reality rather than merely represent it: pressing on its walls until something answers from the other side. Pynchon remains my favourite living writer. After *Solenoid*, he no longer stands there alone.
