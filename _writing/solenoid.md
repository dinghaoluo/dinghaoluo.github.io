<span class="dropcap">L</span>ice came before the fourth dimension in Cărtărescu's ***Solenoid***. The novel opens at the protagonist's scalp, where metaphysics touches skin. Before the buried solenoids and levitating bodies, there is a schoolteacher over a sink, combing lice from his hair and noticing, with horrible calm, the shared organic indignity of their bodies and his. Revelation grows from itch; cosmic escape begins as infestation. Only after that can the house lift its sleepers, the school grow corridors like mazes, the city press its wounds and machinery through its residents' skin.

The book makes its first metaphysical claim in this humiliating register. The surreal and the metaphysical stem from the real and physical: the scalp, the belly button, the tooth, the classroom, the tramline, the blind wall of a neighbour's house. *Surrealism* is too clean a word for this. It assumes a stable reality later breached by dream. *Solenoid* begins from a harsher suspicion: reality is only the portion of strangeness that adult life has trained us to tolerate.

Cărtărescu gives the key in a sentence so plain it almost hides its violence: 'Nothing is strange to a child, because he lives in the strange.' The sentence gives this piece its title because it describes the book more precisely than any genre label. Childhood, in *Solenoid*, has little to do with innocence, nostalgia, or a decorative reservoir of wonder. It is a cognitive condition before the world has been sorted into permissible and impermissible categories; before dream, memory, shame, illness, hallucination, and fact have been assigned to separate drawers. The adult mind, schooled by language, fear, work, embarrassment, and the ordinary police force of other people, later calls that sorting sanity. Cărtărescu writes from before the drawers close.

> Strangeness comes first;<br>
> adulthood teaches us which parts of it to call normal.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact .pullquote--wide}

Mark Fisher becomes useful only after that sentence has changed the problem. The impossible is an adult name for a reality whose strangeness has been reduced to tolerable scale. The loose category of surrealism only gets one so far because it still thinks in terms of breach: reality here, impossibility there. In [*The Weird and the Eerie* (2016)](/thoughts/#the-weird-and-the-eerie), Fisher names failures in our categories: the weird marks the arrival of something that does not belong; the eerie turns on presence, absence, and agency. *Solenoid* moves through both. Irina floating above the bed is weird because the room has admitted an impossible physical law; the hidden solenoids beneath the city are eerie because Bucharest suddenly feels organised by an agency no one above the ground can quite locate. Yet Cărtărescu shifts both terms back toward childhood. The impossible events arrive less as intrusions than as belated evidence that the world was never settled.

<hr class="section-break">

The failure of categories was also my first experience of the book. Before reading *Solenoid*, I had known Cărtărescu mostly as a Nobel-rumour name, which is to say I knew him as a category rather than a writer. I expected a large Eastern European novel whose ambitions could be named before its sentences were felt: late-communist Bucharest, failed literary vocation, childhood, memory, the ordinary swelling toward metaphysical consequence. That description is technically correct, yet it leaves out the texture that makes the book so violent to one's categories: the lice, the school corridors, the factory-hospital, the button above Irina's bed, the Picketists screaming before the mortuary, the Voynich manuscript passing through the archive like a dream-object with paperwork. *Solenoid* makes ordinary life look like a bad reduction of experience, a low-resolution export from a wilder original.

The obvious comparison begins with maximalism, a word that names scale before direction. Pynchon becomes useful at that point. Cărtărescu himself has said, in an [International Booker interview](https://thebookerprizes.com/the-booker-library/features/mircea-cartarescu-and-sean-cotter-interview-solenoid), that *V.* (1963) was the book that impressed him most when he first began writing literature. The comparison stops being biographical almost immediately. Both writers have the maximalist nerve to treat the novel as a total form: joke, archive, dream, machine, body, political ruin, mystical system, all passing through the same circuitry. Scale tempts the comparison; direction breaks it open. Pynchon pushes outward into military infrastructure, corporations, cartels, rockets, information systems, technologies of domination. Cărtărescu pushes inward, into the phenomenology of being trapped inside a body and a city one cannot quite verify as real. Pynchon gives paranoia its great comic physics: everything connects, and connection itself becomes terrifying. Cărtărescu's terror is insufficiency.

Cărtărescu's world does not feel false. It feels too thin for the pressure inside it, as though another version of existence were pressing against this one, separated from us by skin, language, architecture, and some missing word we were never taught to pronounce. The rest of the book gives that absence geography.

Bucharest is where that pressure becomes spatial. Cărtărescu has said, in a [2022 interview](https://theuntranslated.wordpress.com/2022/10/06/mircea-cartarescus-interview-for-de-reactor-on-solenoid-mites-butterflies-kafka-untranslatability-censorship-a-paradise-in-ruins-and-his-latest-novel-theodoros/), that he recreated and invented Bucharest, making it an alter ego, a twin, a state of mind. For once, the authorial gloss locates the pressure without shrinking it. The book's Bucharest has no stable realistic substrate underneath the marvels. It is a remembered city, distorted by attention, fear, repetition, childhood scale, bodily discomfort, and the obscure emotional weather of place.

That remembered city keeps becoming anatomical. The boat-shaped house, the hidden solenoids, Irina levitating above the bed, Palamar's library of mites, the school that expands into nightmarish mazes: each scene feels like a local exposure of the city's tissue. When the narrator follows tramlines to the city's edge, or moves through school corridors that feel secreted rather than built, Bucharest stops behaving like setting and starts behaving like exposed tissue; its topography becomes indistinguishable from the topography of thought.

Magical realism feels too placid as a frame for this. It leaves the critic politely counting layers: reality below, magic above, coexistence announced as if coexistence explained anything. *Solenoid* dissolves the layering. Memory behaves like an unreliable imaging technique; the narrator behaves like both subject and lesion. The city is not simply perceived by a damaged consciousness, nor is consciousness merely projected onto the city. The two systems keep passing through each other until the boundary between perception and world becomes diagnostically useless.

Instability this deep soon spreads into knowledge. Reading *Solenoid*, one repeatedly loses confidence in the boundary between Cărtărescu's inventions and the historical record. The Voynich manuscript is the perfect Cărtărescu object: real yet untranslatable, saturated with meaning that can never be confirmed. Then Charles Howard Hinton appears, then *The Gadfly*, then fourth-dimensional geometry, then occult genealogies of science and literature, and the archive begins to feel oneiric without becoming fake. Pynchon performs a related operation, filling fiction with realia until fact itself seems conspiratorial. In *Solenoid*, the facts do stranger, quieter damage. They feel like objects that have passed through dream and returned with their surfaces changed.

The same doubling reaches biography. In the novel, the narrator's epic poem, *The Fall*, is humiliated at the Workshop of the Moon and he becomes a schoolteacher. In [Federico Perelmuter's account](https://astra-mag.com/articles/this-invented-nation/), the fictional *The Fall* shadows Cărtărescu's *The Levant*: the real workshop reception was rapturous where the narrator's is ruinous. The inversion belongs to the same logic as Bucharest itself: a doubled city finds its narrator in a doubled life. The book is built from the branch that did not happen, or perhaps from the life that literature's institutions refused to certify.

The cheap version would make failure a romance, with rejection purifying the artist and success made vulgar by comparison. Cărtărescu refuses that consolation. Failure makes his narrator porous: open to resentment, revelation, tenderness, grandiosity, self-pity, disgust, and forms of attention that a cleaner life might have sealed off. The novel became uncomfortably personal for me at the scene of humiliation, where rejection turns into public misclassification: a room deciding what kind of person the narrator is allowed to become. I distrust institutional vindication even when I want it, perhaps especially when I want it; *Solenoid* understands how quickly an accident hardens into a biography. Its counterfactual offers no revenge of the misunderstood genius. It makes both success and failure harder to aestheticise.

The archive might make the novel sound like a system of signs. The body prevents that escape. In a 2025 interview with *Chicago Review*, Cărtărescu explicitly invokes [*soma sema*](https://www.chicagoreview.org/soma-sema-an-interview-with-mircea-cartarescu/), the body as prison. I do not believe in that clean separation. Mind happens as body, through history, environment, appetite, injury, and other people. Yet the phenomenology of imprisonment is real. One can know, abstractly, that mind and body are not separate substances, and still feel the insult of embodiment: a capacity for infinity tied to skin, sweat, spinal pain, teeth, infection, appetite, decay.

Here the body-horror becomes structural: the body gives the metaphysical problem its evidence and its insult. Metaphysics never escapes tissue. Lice, mites, sweat, skin, navels, dental shame, bodily fluids, illness, the obscene smallness of organisms: Cărtărescu keeps dragging transcendence back through pores. The fourth dimension matters because the third has teeth, pores, sweat, infection. The desire to escape existence is generated by existence at its most intimate.

If disgust asks how one might escape the body, the house-on-fire dilemma asks what escape is worth when another body remains inside the burning room. Cărtărescu describes the decisive choice as one between saving a masterpiece and saving a child; the narrator chooses the child. In the same interview, Cărtărescu links that choice to solidarity, to the refusal to save only oneself. If the body is a prison, it is at least a prison shared with children, lovers, students, the sick, the ridiculous Picketists in their mortuary of protest, the dead whose traces continue to structure the living. Their picketing is absurd on the surface, yet the absurdity clarifies the point: even metaphysical revolt has to borrow the forms of collective protest, placards and gatherings and bodies standing together in a room. No one exits alone without turning salvation into another cruelty.

<hr class="section-break">

By this point, labels have become bad instruments. They point without measuring. Childhood, Bucharest, failure, archive, body, salvation: they recur as loops in one coil, each recurrence making clean diagnosis less useful. Hooyman's [labyrinth](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/a-romanian-daedalus-surrealist-labyrinth-on-mircea-cartarescus-solenoid/) remains the right spatial metaphor for bewilderment. The title's object adds the missing physics.

The solenoid under the house matters first as an object: the thing itself, the hum beneath the ordinary, the force that warps the floorboards and lifts a woman from her bed. The device also gives the novel its syntax. A solenoid is a coil that produces a magnetic field when current passes through it; Cărtărescu builds narrative current the same way, by making return generate force. At first the returns seem almost formal: episode coils around episode, dream around memory, the fictional Bucharest around the historical one. Then the loops begin to carry ethical and conceptual friction. Failed life coils around successful life; science around mysticism; disgust around tenderness; the longing to leave the body around the child who must be saved inside it. The movement is induction rather than ordinary progression. Each return alters what the next return can mean, until distinctions that seemed stable begin to tilt. The boundary between perception and world becomes diagnostically useless because the novel has been training one's categories to fail.

> A labyrinth traps by passage;<br>
> a solenoid charges by repetition.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact}

The same principle explains why summary fails. The book's true unit is the charged scene: the poetry humiliation, the classrooms, the endless corridors, the factory-like hospital, the tram reaching the edge of the city, the impossible domestic machinery of the house, Palamar's mites under the lens, the sense that Bucharest has always had an underside waiting for the right pressure. Even the most outlandish images retain a practical texture: a button pressed, a ceiling opening, a route followed, a body placed under observation. These scenes do not assemble into an argument in a linear way. They alter the reader's conductivity. By the time the larger metaphysical machinery becomes visible, explanation has given way to induction.

If the book's meanings keep pressing against available language, translation becomes one of its metaphysical tests. Sean Cotter's English is inseparable from that pressure. I do not read Romanian; my Cărtărescu is necessarily Cotter's Cărtărescu in English, and the dependence matters. A novel like this could die in English in several ways: all ornament, no pulse; all archive, no fever; all strangeness smoothed into prestige-literary mist. Cotter keeps the sentences dense without making them airless. He also preserves inquiry, the sense that every object has to be touched, worried, opened, suspected.

I feel this partly as a translator. The title sentence has to sound almost childishly plain while carrying a metaphysical insult; much of the book asks for that doubleness. The English keeps scholarship and hallucination in friction. Translation belongs to the metaphysics itself because the whole book is obsessed with missing words, unreadable manuscripts, and meanings that press against the available language without fully entering it.

By the end, the book had altered my tolerance for incompleteness. The Voynich manuscript, the fourth dimension, the hidden solenoids, the failed poem, the city's underside, the body's prison, the missing word in the riddle of the world: all of them circle the same absence. The question that gives the whole book its gravitational pull is brutally simple: how does one get saved? *Solenoid* never answers by filling the absence. It withholds the word and makes the withholding physical.

*Solenoid* is one of the great reading experiences of my life. Pynchon remains the writer who first taught me to love fictional totality; Cărtărescu has made that territory less solitary. Across nearly seven hundred pages, the book restored an embarrassing suspicion: press hard enough on reality's walls and something might answer from the other side.
