King's Cross lost power, and nearly every train to London was cancelled. I sat on the only Greater Anglia service still running, the carriage packed with bodies. Across from me was a man with a London accent rushing to make a half-seven dinner; to my left a girl with green hair and a nose ring; diagonally opposite sat an older woman, possibly East Asian, speaking to the middle-aged man standing beside her in a Beijing accent. I pulled out the mango and strawberries I had grabbed at M&S and tore open the plastic. Outside, a downpour started and stopped within minutes; a patch of blue sky showed through to the north. After several minutes, the board showed that even this sole surviving service was delayed. The Sithu Aye gig I had been waiting two months for had no landing. The smell of sweat in the carriage was stinging. I threaded through the crowd, refunded my ticket at the counter, and went home.

Counting from the start of July, this was the sixth Friday. I had been in Paulsen's lab for six weeks; my patch clamp work was beginning to show results. After five solid weeks of training, I finally recorded my first long-term potentiation on Wednesday. Eric Kandel says this is the molecular mechanism of memory. Whether he is right or not, it was a cause for genuine happiness. I told my partner; after days of frustration I finally had something to show. She said, see, things are not that bad.

But the uncontrollable things are still too many. After Wednesday's success came two consecutive days of deflation. Thursday's brain slices were in poor health; the neurons died almost immediately. Friday I had to leave early for London and ended up skulking home empty-handed. I asked Tanja if I could come in over the weekend; she said I needed her supervision, but she was unwell and probably would not come. Cells do not cooperate when forced. In matters like these, the scientific enterprise wears the veil of individualism and postmodernism. As Kandel put it in *In Search of Memory*: 'Nobody imagined that scientists might actually have lives of their own!'

Over the past few weeks this sense of the personal and contingent had been taking root. First the realisation that I had one month before PhD applications opened; then the realisation that I did not want to be a post-doc. The eager anticipation of an academic career was being crushed by the chaotic noise of the internet; reaching out to the doctoral students around me, everyone seemed equally unsure about their future. For a moment it felt as though the PhD would be the last bubble of the scientific dream, and that after receiving the title, the weight of living would close around the cat's throat. The ivory tower extends only six floors upward, and already I could picture the disorientation at a hundred metres.

This is a resentful, dispiriting kind of helplessness. Idealism is nowhere to be found at altitude; the scientific dream is crushed by the thirty-thousand-pound pre-tax average salary of a UK biology post-doc. I thought about this while combing my hair in the morning, caught my own resolute stare in the mirror, and did not know whether to laugh. A few days earlier, browsing UCL PhD programmes, a sudden wasteland feeling had swept in: all sense of purpose stripped away in an instant, hovering over the desk, inside the coffee cup, but unable to see my own reflection. This anxiety may be temporary, but it is also real. Avoidance is perhaps shameful, but it is certainly useless. Alongside the anxiety came a sudden clarity: I had already walked far enough down this road, reaching the eve of graduation, thinking seriously about life planning for the first time. And this life planning was no longer the childhood kind, 'I want to be a scientist.' It was planning for survival: 'without sufficient money, one cannot live well.' In a capitalist nation, life is not about motion but about shouldering one's duty as a cog. Perhaps I had always known this, but now the duty felt unbearably real, and desperate. Whether it is the scientific enterprise veiled by individualism and postmodernism, or capitalism veiled over the cat's eyes: many people understand, many people say nothing. What remains is a crowd of brothers and sisters feeling their way across the river on stones, offering their lives to the current, leaving behind a gorgeous spectacle of sacrifice for those who follow.

I did not make it to London this afternoon, and the soba I had been promised went uneaten. Hungry in the evening; my partner said she wanted watermelon. I thought, that is good: a clear objective. I have none; I want to eat everything. I remembered the small plate of mixed nuts served alongside the noodles at Tokyo Diner: cashews, green peas, chickpeas, almonds, walnuts, macadamias, pistachios. Not knowing which to eat first, I always spooned them up in small mixed bites. The lighting at Tokyo Diner is always warm; it falls on the back of the neck and makes the heart feel warm too. My partner likes cold soba; I hope to take her there before long.

The wind does not stop at night, threading in from the edges of the half-open window, settling where it pleases in the warm room.
