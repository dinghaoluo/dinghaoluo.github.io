<span class="dropcap">T</span>his year, especially, I felt the pandemic's most direct effects. From the visa for leaving to start a PhD, to the wish to reunite with the person I love and with my family, too many things in life had already been put on hold for a long time, then pushed farther and farther out of reach by one variant after another. We rocked by boat past so many harbours of expectation. On shore stood crowds of masked people, all waiting for the world to answer their calls.

Thinking about it, it has now been more than two years. I still remember the start of the pandemic. On 31 January last year, the WHO said in a tweet that the number of deaths in China had reached 170: "We must remember that these are people, not numbers." More than a year has passed, and now that daily death counts can reach the tens of thousands, what remains is mostly numbness and powerlessness.

Whenever I think about this, I feel that I am still one of the lucky ones. However much control I have lost over life, I still have the company of the person I love, of family and friends. There are still people who listen to my voice, still people to whom my life matters. Thinking of this, I feel I have no standing to complain inside this chaos, no right to pretend that the damage to me has been vast and ruinous. But anxiety and fear have a shape. Each time I look at flights home, each time I look at the entry policies of one region or country after another, each time I see people in the airport not wearing masks, that tangible anxiety and fear wells up like water that cannot be stopped. The helplessness carries enormous energy. It shatters many attachments and hopes. You start to ask, "Why does no one care?" "Could this be our future?" There is no answer, but faintly you sense that the answer is already inside us. No one dares say it.

Sometimes I think a hurricane, an earthquake, or a wildfire burning through an entire city might be better than this. Life and death close enough to touch, separation close enough to see, feel more worthy of grief and anger than a rain of small, scattered disasters. But the pandemic is not like that. It is not smoke, and it is not a tornado. This kind of small disaster falls on each of us one drop at a time. I want to speak of hope, but for a hundred miles in every direction I cannot see any.

Shanji and Amo wish everyone a happy new year. :)
