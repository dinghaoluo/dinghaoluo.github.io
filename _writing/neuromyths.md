'Men are from Mars, women are from Venus.' 'We only use 10% of our brains.' 'Left-brain people are logical; right-brain people are creative.' Myths about the brain and the mind are everywhere, and no amount of debunking seems to slow their spread. Pop-psychology bestsellers and corporate training programmes recycle them with abandon, each iteration drifting further from the evidence it claims to summarise. The myths endure not because the science is ambiguous but because the stories are satisfying: they offer tidy explanations for messy human experience. The question is not merely where the myths go wrong, but why they persist, and what it takes to see through them.

## The 10% brain

The human brain accounts for roughly 3% of total body mass yet consumes about 20% of total energy intake. Despite this extravagance, the claim that we use only 10% of it has proved remarkably durable. In *Lucy*, the protagonist unlocks the remaining 90% and becomes superhuman. But even she would struggle to explain why natural selection would maintain, at enormous metabolic cost, an organ that is 90% inert. From an evolutionary standpoint, tissue that contributes nothing to survival and reproduction is tissue that should have been selected against long ago.

Barry Gordon, a neurologist at Johns Hopkins, has argued that the myth's appeal lies in its flattering implication: the notion that vast untapped potential lurks inside each of us, and that our failures are simply failures of access. One cannot remember vocabulary, finish assignments, or parse a paper; it must be because 90% of the brain is sitting idle. This is comforting, but it has no scientific basis, and it actively undermines science communication by displacing evidence-based accounts of how cognition actually works.

The evidence against the myth is overwhelming. Virtually any lesion to brain tissue produces measurable cognitive impairment; if 90% of the brain were unused, damage to that 90% should be consequence-free. Functional imaging studies consistently show that most cognitive tasks activate far more than 10% of neural tissue. Post-mortem examinations of healthy brains do not reveal large-scale neuronal degeneration of the kind one would expect if most neurons were chronically idle. In fact, during ordinary waking activity, one uses nearly 100% of the brain. Even during sleep, the prefrontal cortex and primary somatosensory cortex remain active, and these two regions alone exceed 10% of total brain volume. [1] Our potential does not come from 'unlocking' dormant neural real estate; it comes from the brain's continuous capacity for reorganisation and refinement.

## Left brain, right brain

Whether the two hemispheres of the brain serve different functions is a question neuroscience calls 'lateralisation'. And it is true that some functions are lateralised. Language is the most established example. Over a century ago, two French physicians identified regions in the left frontal and temporal lobes, now named Broca's area and Wernicke's area, that are intimately involved in the production and comprehension of speech. [2] Patients with damage to Broca's area often cannot produce fluent speech; when attempting to say 'I want coffee', they might manage only 'I. Coffee. Want.' Patients with damage to Wernicke's area cannot comprehend what others say. That language function resides predominantly in the left hemisphere has long been the consensus.

But the consensus has grown more nuanced. In recent decades, researchers have recognised that language lateralisation correlates with handedness. In left-handed individuals, Broca's and Wernicke's areas sometimes form in the right hemisphere; in some left-handers and ambidextrous people, language shows no lateralisation at all. A study published in *Brain* found that as the degree of left-handedness increases, so does the probability of right-hemisphere language dominance. [3] If even language, the textbook example of lateralisation, does not follow a universal rule, what hope is there for more complex cognitive functions?

The strongest evidence for lateralisation comes from split-brain research conducted in the 1960s and after. The corpus callosum, the axonal bundle connecting the two hemispheres, was severed in certain patients for surgical reasons, allowing researchers to study each hemisphere in isolation. These studies found that the left hemisphere is primarily responsible for analytical processing and verbal expression, while the right hemisphere handles spatial information and aesthetic appreciation. [4] But generalising these findings to the intact brain is unwise. In most people, the corpus callosum is intact, and the two hemispheres communicate continuously; this is fundamentally different from the split-brain condition. Moreover, real-world cognitive tasks are rarely as neatly bounded as laboratory stimuli: analysis can be applied to a passage of text or a painting, and most everyday tasks require the coordinated effort of both hemispheres. [5]

As Christian Jarrett has argued in *Psychology Today*, the 'left-brain logical, right-brain creative' formulation has a seductive simplicity that lets people sort themselves into camps. [6] The sorting may be comforting, but it is wrong. And the comfort itself may be the point: the myth survives not on evidence but on the human appetite for self-categorisation.

## Gendered cognition

'Boys are suited to science; girls are suited to the arts.' This perennial claim smuggles in an assumption: that men are innately logical thinkers and women are innately creative ones. Recent decades of research have made clear that even where male and female brains differ, the differences lie in anatomical structure, not in cognitive kind, and what the structural differences actually mean remains far from settled. [7]

Using diffusion tensor imaging, researchers have observed that intra-hemispheric and intra-regional connections are more common in male brains, while inter-hemispheric and inter-regional connections are more common in female brains. The research team suggested that male brains are optimised for perception-action coordination, while female brains favour communication between regions responsible for analytical processing. [8] But this interpretation overlooks a confound: male brains are, on average, larger. Some researchers argue that the observed connectivity differences are largely a consequence of differences in brain volume, which create different structural and metabolic demands. A study from Hanyang University in South Korea supports this: as brain volume increases, cortical thickening is minimal, but the complexity of cortical folding increases substantially. [9] Brains of different sizes cannot be directly compared without accounting for the different demands that different volumes impose.

There is a deeper problem. The brain is highly plastic: connections that are 'specified' at birth are continuously remodelled by experience. An adult's connectome therefore cannot serve as evidence for innate cognitive sex differences; it is as much a product of environment as of biology. Anne Fausto-Sterling, of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, has argued that differential social treatment of men and women, including differences in education and working environments, likely contributes to shaping whatever neuroanatomical differences are observed. [10] When interpreting neuroscientific data, the gender trap is always close at hand.

## The Mozart effect

Classical music, and Mozart's piano sonatas in particular, have long been yoked to the mythology of early childhood development. The so-called 'Mozart effect' claims that infants and young children can boost their intelligence by listening to such music, thereby 'winning at the starting line'. When the authors of *Mind Myths* visited an Italian farm, the farmer proudly declared that his cows had been raised on classical music; it made them produce more milk.

The claim traces to a 1993 *Nature* paper. Psychologists at the University of Wisconsin gave three groups of participants different treatments: one group listened to Mozart's *Sonata for Two Pianos in D major, K. 448*; a second listened to relaxation instructions; a third sat in silence for ten minutes. The Mozart group showed a temporary improvement in spatial reasoning that disappeared within fifteen minutes. [11] As the paper's profile rose, a global craze for Mozart ensued. But subsequent studies found the result difficult to replicate: Mozart's music did not reliably improve infants' spatial cognition. Other research showed that any music the listener enjoyed produced similar transient gains. [12, 13]

A meta-analysis published in *Intelligence* found that many of the studies reporting significant effects had financial or professional ties to the original research team, further diminishing the claim's credibility. [14] The truth is probably mundane: listening to Mozart, or Bach, or anything one finds stimulating, produces a modest and temporary boost in cognitive performance relative to silence. Stimulation improves performance; this has been known for a long time and requires no special theory of musical genius.

## On persistence

The neuromyths discussed here are far from the only ones in circulation, and their origins are not always straightforward. Some arise from oversimplification of real findings; others from academic misconduct or failures of replication. But regardless of origin, their effects are corrosive. They infiltrate genuine, evidence-based information and dilute its authority. Myths like 'men and women think fundamentally differently' can reinforce existing stereotypes, harm the interests of both sexes, and distort social policy.

Combating neuromyths requires not only continued progress in research but a willingness, on the part of each of us, to understand the brain on its own terms rather than reaching for a convenient 'scientific' excuse for the things we cannot do.
