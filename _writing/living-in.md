<span class="dropcap">C</span>ambridge is a town of dim evenings. My tutor mentioned this within days of arrival. His name was Stuart Martin, both halves equally plausible as a first name, and for three years I never stopped confusing them. Around the 5th or 6th of October 2017 (a detail of no conceivable importance), he told us to fit front and rear lights to our bicycles and wear helmets, especially after dark. In Cambridge, street lamps are a scarce resource.

Naturally, as a university student of keen observational powers, I did not need Dr Martin's warning to notice the darkness. I still remember the first evening I arrived, late September 2017, sharing a taxi from Heathrow with three classmates. Outside the window: the London skyline in passing, planes descending low to land, dark fields and woods, the villages on the fringe of Cambridgeshire, and finally Cambridge's centuries-old buildings. The driver dropped me outside Westminster College (it would be a year before I learned this was not a Cambridge college). I dragged one large and one small suitcase, shouldering a rucksack of at least five kilograms, and delivered my body to the gates of Magdalene, the Cat College, where I rang the porters' lodge bell.

Half past midnight. I stood at the gates, surrounded by an oily amber dimness that seeped downhill, saturating Cambridge's night.

That evening I slept in one of Magdalene's rare guest rooms. Two beds. I threw the rucksack onto whichever one I arbitrarily decided not to sleep in, showered, and slept. The next morning I walked the narrow lanes towards the town centre, stopping for breakfast at the Costa outside Sainsbury's (since closed). Still unable to operate the SLR, I shot the scattered yellow lights on the Cam at an ISO above 10,000.

No student arriving at university for the first time can anticipate how brief three years will be. Gradually, the amber light of mornings and evenings, a blue mountain bike, a Totoro plush, two iPads, three textbooks, and several dozen handouts composed a debris-field of memory, threaded through the absurd ideology of an undergraduate.

February 2018, the first winter, the first snowfall I had seen in Britain. A white expanse that merged with (rather than buried) the lawns, streets and old stone along the Cam. From the clamour of the market square to the quiet of Magdalene's library: five minutes. In those five minutes I considered what to have for dinner. A consecutive string of answers reading 'mapo tofu rice' stitched together the Lent and Easter terms of first year. After dinner came the library, where life ran from seven in the evening to one in the morning, sometimes two or three, until the snow carpeted Magdalene's courts.

I still remember the philosophy supervisions: one per week, one three-thousand-word essay per supervision. My favourite question (if memory serves) was: '"The King of France is bald." Is this statement true or false?' I wrote from Russell to Strawson, then onwards to Alexius Meinong, whom the lectures had never mentioned, and then (driven by inexplicable enthusiasm) spent a small fortune on Amazon ordering a book about Meinong. His account of 'existence' struck me at the time as vastly superior to Russell's. Fortunately, a similar essay question appeared in the first-year exams; I wrote on it, as a matter of course, and felt happy, as a matter of course.

I mention the essays for the nights they produced: falling asleep face-down in the library on multiple occasions, and going to Sainsbury's at eleven at night for coffee. These experiences, for me, may matter considerably more than the follicular condition of the King of France.

Second year brought unexpected elements. Running, for instance. My room was no longer on Magdalene's main site but in Cripps Court, the door opening directly onto the Cam. In the early morning the river retained the smell and sound of night. Following it, one could run to an open meadow. I never learned its name; I remember only that at the centre of that expanse stood a circular iron plate, and that all the smells and sounds left over from the night dispersed there. Sometimes, reaching the meadow, I would stand still and wait for the sun to rise in the distance. Other times, being a person with essays due, I had no right to such useless romance and would run back to the room within ten minutes for breakfast.

Other second-year memories belong to Castle Hill. The clouds and I divided the labour. I was responsible for sitting on the hill watching people come and go, along with the occasional dog or cat. The clouds were responsible for drifting. We both had bright futures.

On Castle Hill there was also the only pub I had visited in Cambridge at the time, called The Architect. In that pub I (together with Wu Que and Julia) drank my first mouthful of beer. There was also something called 'mulled wine', which I never tasted again after second year.

Sitting at the long desk against the wall in my room, I sometimes thought about the meaning of recording. To say simply 'to leave a trace' feels like a dereliction of duty towards the complexity of human desire. As I have written before, the act of recording is itself diverse. Some recordings intend to escape the subject; some intend to distort fact; some intend to expose a viewpoint. Looking back, my own intent has shifted many times. Now it leans closest to 'exposing a viewpoint', corresponding to a postmodern stance.

The most frequent instrument of that recording is my camera (a D90 from over a decade ago, a gift from my father). From second year onwards I realised that behind the viewfinder sits more than a right eye: a viewpoint containing ideology, thought-systems, emotion, reason, and a multitude of other factors. This viewpoint corresponds to the 'I' at the moment the shutter is pressed; yet once the shutter closes and opens, the image it captures separates from the dynamic 'I' and becomes an independent record. Among these records are the seascapes of Whitby and Glasgow station from second year, the Florida sunset and London's Chinatown from third. For me, these photographs record the external world at that instant alongside the 'I' and viewpoint of that moment.

Life in this dim town ended abruptly in March. On the 21st, before the evening darkness could settle over Magdalene's rooftops, I took a taxi from Cambridge to Heathrow, stealing a last look at the town in a rush. Wu Que walked me to the pick-up point and embraced me. The car arrived. The driver sprayed disinfectant on my suitcase, on my clothes, on my shoes, then turned the nozzle towards Wu Que and asked if he was also getting in. He said no. The driver said right. I said well, I'm off then. Wu Que said okay. I got in. The driver got in. The car pulled away. I wept quietly in the back seat. It was an unremarkable day. I recorded that unremarkable day with my camera. 'What happens next?' 'What will happen then?' 'Will the exams go ahead as normal?' 'Will the PhD still start on time?' The viewpoint behind the viewfinder was on the verge of shattering.

The act of recording is endless, but the viewpoint is not. It changes like a person. Looking back now at three years in Cambridge, gazing towards the horizon of tomorrow, new feelings arrive. The damp-return days always bring strange dreams; in them I see the Cambridge town at night again, the river threading through Magdalene, St John's, Trinity, King's, Clare, Queens', running south from north, flowing towards morning.

> The act of recording is endless, but the viewpoint is not.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact}

I think of one person running along the Cam towards the sun about to rise, looking down from Castle Hill at the people and traffic ascending and descending. Before long, living elsewhere again.
