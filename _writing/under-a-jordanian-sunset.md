<span class="dropcap">T</span>o walk from downtown Amman to the ruins of the Roman citadel, one has to climb several slopes that are not especially easy. People's houses crowd thickly along them. Now and then a cat darts out, slips lightly past our feet, and vanishes into the shadow of a wall. It is low season, with few tourists. The people moving up and down the slope are people inside ordinary life: groups of three or five carrying gas canisters; others holding fresh fruit and vegetables; children running past in packs, laughing and talking. A few stopped suddenly in front of us, eyes wide, and tried out a halting "ni hao" in Chinese. Their childish voices carried some part of the old city's vitality.

This is the living face of a thousand-year-old city. It was nearly the hour. We stood on the slope and looked down at the shrunken city below: the thousand-year-old theatre leaning silently against the city's edge, the forest of century-old buildings crowding the view in a slightly disorderly posture. Then the broadcast began. The long call to prayer covered the whole city in an instant, and even the air seemed to slow. Only that evening, while eating at a restaurant, did we suddenly realise that when the call to prayer begins, the music of the whole city has to stop. The swollen city seemed to be gathered by the call into a single still point, then, following that long melody, slowly spread outward with a faint glow.

The feeling appears everywhere in Jordan. Among the deep red stone walls of Petra, the theatre sits like a mark carved by time, still there two thousand years later. In the vast desert of Wadi Rum, the wine-red sunset has been like this for tens of thousands of years, unchanged. Once, Lawrence of Arabia made camp in Wadi Rum's damp valleys; now we sit quietly under the stars on this same land, watching from afar as the red light of the sun slowly rises from the horizon. A point of light, scattering its faint glow outward with the song.

Standing on Amman's Roman ruins, watching the shadows of the hills gradually cover the city's yellow brick and the thousands of years of history held by this Middle Eastern city, from camels on the Silk Road to refugees from Palestine, from the ambition of Arab unity to today's merchants and travellers, I felt history's current gather and disperse here, carrying an uncertain echo. Now the ancient city is gone. Who knows when the great wave of history will return.
