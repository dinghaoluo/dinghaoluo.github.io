<span class="dropcap">I</span>n October 1650, the Massachusetts General Court ordered a small theological tract burned in the Boston marketplace. Its author, [William Pynchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Pynchon), was the founder of Springfield, Massachusetts and one of the more prosperous of the first Puritan settlers. William Pynchon, in his book *The Meritorious Price of Our Redemption*, had argued a doctrine about Christ's atonement that the Bay Colony's ministers found intolerable, which earned the book its later reputation as [one of the first books banned and burned in British North America](https://publicdomainreview.org/essay/the-price-of-suffering-william-pynchon-and-the-meritorious-price-of-our-redemption/). Pynchon was summoned before the General Court, offered a partial retraction, and then sailed for England in 1652, never returning to the Colonies. Three hundred and twenty-three years later, his descendant Thomas Pynchon dropped that incident, lightly disguised, into the middle of *Gravity's Rainbow* (1973). The protagonist Tyrone Slothrop's first American ancestor, William Slothrop, is said in Part III to have written a tract called *On Preterition* that was 'among the first books to've been not only banned but also ceremonially burned in Boston'. In it, William Slothrop argued 'holiness for these "second Sheep," without whom there'd be no elect'; and, more provocatively, that 'what Jesus was for the elect, Judas Iscariot was for the Preterite'. The Boston Elect, the narrator tells us, 'were pissed off about that'.

Now, what exactly are 'Elect' and 'Preterite'? John Calvin, the sixteenth-century reformer whose theology the Bay Colony Puritans inherited, defined the doctrine in his [*Institutes of the Christian Religion*](https://ccel.org/ccel/calvin/institutes.v.xxii.html) (final Latin edition 1559; trans. Henry Beveridge, 1845). Predestination is 'the eternal decree of God, by which he determined with himself whatever he wished to happen with regard to every man'; some are preordained to eternal life, others to eternal damnation, and those whom God dooms to destruction are 'excluded from access to life by a just and blameless, but at the same time incomprehensible, judgment'. Calvin's own term for the damned is 'reprobate'; *Preterite*, from Latin *praeteritus* ('passed over'), is the word Pynchon chose for the novel, and it carries a slightly different charge: where Calvin's reprobation is deliberate exclusion, Pynchon's 'passed over' implies something closer to administrative oblivion, not having been noticed at all. The emotional difference belongs to the novel, not to Calvin. This is an administrative pre-sorting: God has decided already, and the earthly world is a display case for a decision made outside time. Moral conduct, no matter how noble, cannot interfere.

The German sociologist Max Weber, in the essays that became *[The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/weber/protestant-ethic/ch04.htm)* (published in *Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik*, 1904–05; trans. Talcott Parsons, 1930), describes how a theology of pre-sorting produced, over time, a whole shape of everyday economic life. Predestination creates, for the Calvinist, 'a feeling of unprecedented inner loneliness of the single individual': there is nothing one can do to alter the eternal decree, no priest or sacrament to intercede, and therefore one's whole life becomes a search for evidence of election. Weber's Puritan finds that evidence in systematic worldly activity, constructing 'a life of good works combined into a unified system' by which he can distinguish himself, moment by moment, from the damned. Good works do not purchase salvation; they function as signs, and the signs harden into a worldly discipline that outlasts the theology which produced it. '[Fate decreed](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/weber/protestant-ethic/ch05.htm),' Weber writes in the final chapter, 'that the cloak should become an iron cage.'

This line of inheritance, from Calvin's *Institutes* through William Pynchon's contested tract to Weber's diagnosis of its secular afterlife, is legible throughout *Gravity's Rainbow*. Weber's 'iron cage' reappears at nearly every scale of the novel, and the V-2 rocket is its most literal expression. A rocket's target coordinate is chosen before launch; cutoff settings and angle are calculated on the ground to place the warhead within a predetermined area. At Brennschluss, engine cutoff, the rocket's actual velocity and position determine its ballistic trajectory; everything after that moment is falling into the path those prior calculations prescribed. Dispersion, weather, component failure, and chance determine which body receives the blast. The person beneath has been placed inside a target area before the rocket rises from the launch table; no action on their part can alter the exposure, because the meaningful decision happened elsewhere, in instruments and calculations and the hands of people who will never see the face of the person they destroy. God's eternal decree is made outside time; the rocket's targeting is made before launch. And just as the Calvinist cannot know with certainty whether they are elect or damned, the person beneath the arc cannot hear the rocket coming, because it descends faster than sound; the first evidence of the decision is the blast itself.

<hr class="section-break">

In Calvin's *Institutes* the sorting is the eternal decree; in Weber it is the counting-house and its ledgers; in the novel it is the V-2, which enters *Gravity's Rainbow* by breaking the order in which danger is usually recognised. A conventional bomber raid over London is already terror, but it is terror with stages: engines overhead, sirens, searchlights, the sky briefly turned into an object of fear. The rocket removes the stages. It descends faster than sound. In the novel's opening pages, the intelligence agent Pirate Prentice scans the horizon and the V-2 first appears as 'a vapor trail. Already a finger's width higher now. But not from an airplane. Airplanes are not launched vertically.' Then the trail stops climbing at Brennschluss, engine cutoff. Pirate stares at it and knows what everyone soon learns: 'He won't hear the thing come in. It travels faster than the speed of sound. The first news you get of it is the blast. Then, if you're still around, you hear the sound of it coming in.'

Death from above was already old by 1944; what the V-2 changes is the possibility of encounter. One cannot look back at the pilot, curse the aircraft, listen for the engines, or turn the sky into a legible field of danger. The person under the arc meets a result. The victim receives blast; the world from which that blast originated remains remote, technical, bureaucratic, almost placid. Pointsman, thinking about Slothrop, calls the reversal 'a piece of time neatly snipped out... a few feet of film run backwards... the blast of the rocket, fallen faster than sound— then growing out of it the roar of its own fall, catching up to what's already death and burning... a ghost in the sky'. The meaningful action has already happened elsewhere, in instruments, cut-off settings, launch tables, target maps. Modern violence does not have to look its target in the face. It can move its victims out of sight, turn cities into target areas, and let calculation arrive where encounter used to be; the V-2 needs procedure, not hatred at the point of impact.

Brennschluss, engine cutoff, is where the displaced causality becomes concrete. The V-2's guidance system is technically specific, and Pynchon renders it in unusual detail. Operational V-2s could use an [integrating accelerometer](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/html/trecms/AD1195605/index.html) to trigger engine cutoff at a preset velocity; Helmut Hölzer's [Mischgerät](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mischger%C3%A4t_(V-2_guidance_computer)), a separate stability computer designed in 1941, combined gyro-position signals with rate terms and sent corrective commands to the steering servos during powered flight. Pynchon's novel assigns the rocket a more elaborate guidance: a 'double integration' in which acceleration is summed twice to yield distance, so that the cutoff can be set as a preset distance along the flight path rather than as the historical preset velocity. The passage belongs to the novel's account of advanced IG guidance, and it carries the argument forward whether or not it maps precisely to the historical Mischgerät. Pynchon summarises the whole train, near the start of Part III, with careful engineering pathos: 'To get to distance from acceleration, the Rocket had to integrate twice—needed a moving coil, transformers, electrolytic cell, bridge of diodes, one tetrode... an elaborate dance of design precautions to get to what human eyes saw first of all—the distance along the flight path.' Everything after Brennschluss is ballistic. The rocket falls into a coordinate already chosen before launch. The arc the novel takes its title from is, in this sense, the visible shape of two integrations performed in silence.

Acceleration becomes velocity; velocity becomes distance; distance becomes coordinate. A human being blends, smears, and disappears into the target area before the rocket ever rises from the launch table. Within the novel's account, the guidance system's double integration finds its architectural echo in the underground factory: 'To integrate here', the narrator writes of the Nordhausen tunnels, 'is to operate on a rate of change so that time falls away: change is stilled... The moving vehicle is frozen, in space, to become architecture, and timeless. It was never launched. It will never fall.'

Calculation is not itself the problem. The human brain is poorly equipped for complexity; thinking at any useful scale requires reduction. Spike trains become firing rates, trajectories become variables, populations become rows in a table. My own work is in this tradition: I record from neurons and reduce their output to firing rates, tuning curves, population vectors. When Pynchon's engineers integrate acceleration into distance, the gesture is familiar before the horror is. The moral question begins at the boundary of what a model represents: whether dimensions falling outside representation are also permitted to fall outside responsibility. When the coordinate system cannot represent a prisoner, a daughter, a bird, or a Herero or Nama life, the operator can still keep working; the model keeps producing valid outputs; the boundary becomes a moral instrument. Pynchon does not ask one to become stupid in order to remain ethical. He asks what intelligence becomes under that condition, and the V-2 is his answer: a triumph of applied physics that is also a machine for separating action from recognition.

<hr class="section-break">

If the rocket separates action from recognition, the epistemological question follows: how does one know a weapon that arrives before its own signal? The London rocket strikes are registered in two separate investigations whose data coincide square for square but whose explanations diverge completely: Roger Mexico plots red circles on his Poisson map, tracking statistical distribution; Pointsman, in his laboratory, hunts for a conditioned reflex arc between Slothrop's sexual encounters and the subsequent strikes. Pointsman is the Pavlovian, one of seven co-owners of Pavlov's *[Lectures on Conditioned Reflexes](https://archive.org/details/pavlov-lectures-on-conditioned-reflexes-vol.-1)*, and he cannot survive between zero and one. Pynchon writes the opposition precisely: 'in the domain of zero to one, not-something to something, Pointsman can only possess the zero and the one. He cannot, like Mexico, survive anyplace in between. Like his master I. P. Pavlov before him, he imagines the cortex of the brain as a mosaic of tiny on/off elements. Some are always in bright excitation, others darkly inhibited. The contours, bright and dark, keep changing. But each point is allowed only the two states: waking or sleep. One or zero.' The Pavlovian mind treats the cortex as a switching lattice. Roger Mexico, an anti-Pointsman, treats London as a Poisson field. He does not seek links; he counts.

Mexico's map works. He plots each rocket-strike as a red circle on 576 quarter-square-kilometre squares of London and finds a clean Poisson distribution: given the total number of hits, the equation predicts how many squares will have received none, how many one, two, three, and so on. When Pointsman asks whether the map can tell them which squares are safer, Mexico answers no. He explains the [Monte Carlo Fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gambler%27s_fallacy): 'Bombs are not dogs. No link. No memory. No conditioning.' If the strikes are independent draws from a probability distribution, then no reflex arc, no conditioned stimulus, no Law of Negative Induction connects them, and the Pavlovian's whole cortical map is not a description of the world but an artefact of one man's need for cause. Pointsman is not slow to see the stakes. The narrator gives him the shudder directly: 'How can Mexico play, so at his ease, with these symbols of randomness and fright? Innocent as a child, perhaps unaware—perhaps—that in his play he wrecks the elegant rooms of history, threatens the idea of cause and effect itself. What if Mexico's whole generation have turned out like this? Will Postwar be nothing but "events," newly created one moment to the next? No links? Is it the end of history?'

What Pointsman feels is a specifically modern panic: the fear that a certain kind of statistical thinking, once fully absorbed, will corrode the causal fabric of both science and narrative. The Poisson distribution quietly, neatly sifting among the squares does not tell anyone where the next rocket will land; it predicts aggregate counts, nothing more. There are no antagonists in the model; there is only a mean density. If the reader cannot even connect a rocket to a hater, if the arc is a coordinate and the coordinate is a probability, what is left of the moral universe? But the bureaux that selected London as a target sit upstream of Mexico's model, outside its variables. Someone still chose to send the rocket. The distribution describes impacts accurately and says nothing about the decision to bombard London; the model brackets those prior decisions because they sit outside its variables.

The mathematician Norbert Wiener, at MIT during the war, was contracted by the National Defense Research Committee to solve the problem of anti-aircraft prediction: given a plane trying to evade a shell, how does one compute where the plane will be by the time the shell reaches it? Wiener and Julian Bigelow answered, in a report submitted to the NDRC in February 1942 and circulated as a classified document titled *The Extrapolation, Interpolation and Smoothing of Stationary Time Series* (the 'Yellow Peril' report), by treating the pilot as a servo-mechanism. In a June 1942 summary Wiener wrote: 'The "randomness" or irregularity of an airplane's path is introduced by the pilot... in attempting to force his dynamic craft to execute a useful manoeuver... the pilot behaves like a servo-mechanism, attempting to overcome the intrinsic lag due to the dynamics of his plane as a physical system.' In a 1943 paper co-written with the physiologist [Arturo Rosenblueth](https://sites.evergreen.edu/arunchandra/wp-content/uploads/sites/395/2018/05/teleology.pdf) and Julian Bigelow, Wiener generalised: 'teleological behavior... becomes synonymous with behavior controlled by negative feedback.' By 1948, in *Cybernetics*, the whole framework was public: 'the present age is as truly the age of servo-mechanisms as the nineteenth century was the age of the steam engine'. And by 1954, in the revised edition of *The Human Use of Human Beings*, Wiener had made the ontological move that Peter Galison, in his 1994 essay '[The Ontology of the Enemy](https://monoskop.org/images/9/9c/Galison_Peter_1994_The_Ontology_of_the_Enemy_Norbert_Wiener_and_the_Cybernetic_Vision.pdf)', identifies as the wartime doctrine's true bequest to the postwar world: 'when I give an order to a machine, the situation is not essentially different from that which arises when I give an order to a person'. Galison's argument is that once the enemy pilot has been modelled as a human-machine black box, the same model applies to the allied gunner and, by extension, to anyone whose behaviour might be predicted and corrected.

From February 1960 to September 1962, Thomas Pynchon worked in Boeing's technical-information operation in Seattle, first in Bomarc Service Information and later in Minuteman Field Support; in June 1961 his title changed to technical writer. He appears to have written for the in-house *Bomarc Service News*, an internal technical newsletter for the [CIM-10 Bomarc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CIM-10_Bomarc), a supersonic, ramjet-powered, nuclear-warhead-carrying surface-to-air missile tightly integrated with SAGE, NORAD's automated continental air-defence network. In [Adrian Wisnicki's 2000 rediscovery](https://digitalcommons.unl.edu/englishfacpubs/247) and [Katie Muth's 2019 stylometric analysis](https://doi.org/10.1080/0950236x.2019.1580514) of the *Bomarc Service News* archive, around two dozen unsigned articles have been attributed to Pynchon, including 'The Mad Hatter and the Mercury Wetted Relays', which warned Bomarc technicians that ruptured mercury tubes inside their switch assemblies could drive them insane. Some years later, in *Gravity's Rainbow*, a 'forgotten photographer' would appear as 'a habitue of mercury fumes'. Before *Gravity's Rainbow*, Pynchon had worked inside the documentation of an American cybernetic weapon.

In the novel, the doctrine is voiced, characteristically, by a dead man. Roland Feldspath, 'long-co-opted expert on control systems, guidance equations, feedback situations', speaks to Slothrop from the other side of death as one of the 'invisible Interdictors of the stratosphere'. Citing the fictional *Paranoid Systems of History*, he passes on the speculation that the interwar German inflation may itself have been engineered, 'simply to drive young enthusiasts of the Cybernetic Tradition into Control work: after all, an economy inflating, upward bound as a balloon, its own definition of Earth's surface drifting upward in value, uncontrolled, drifting with the days, the feedback system expected to maintain the value of the mark constant having, humiliatingly, failed'. Then he names the discipline's lullaby: 'Unity gain around the loop, unity gain, zero change, and hush, that way, forever, these were the secret rhymes of the childhood of the Discipline of Control—secret and terrible, as the scarlet histories say. Diverging oscillations of any kind were nearly the Worst Threat.' The Rocket, Feldspath says a few pages later, gets 'reduced, literally, to bourgeois terms, terms of an equation such as that elegant blend of philosophy and hardware, abstract change and hinged pivots of real metals which describes motion under the aspect of yaw control:... preserving, possessing, steering between Scylla and Charybdis the whole way to Brennschluss'. The correspondence Feldspath (and behind him, Pynchon) is drawing runs between a technical practice and a political disposition: an engineer's negative-feedback loop suppresses divergence around a chosen reference value, and the postwar order wants to do the same to markets, populations, and children's playgrounds. The technical fact does not choose the reference value; the political question, which follows and which the technical fact does not answer, is who does. Feldspath sees this only because he has died. 'None of them made the connection, at least not while alive,' the narrator writes; 'it took death to show it to Roland Feldspath, death with its very good chances for being Too Late.'

<hr class="section-break">

Feldspath's Discipline of Control remains abstract until it acquires balance sheets. Pynchon is careful to distinguish 'They' from any particular nation, army, or party. The Firm 'will use anyone, traitors, murderers, perverts, Negroes, even women, to get what They want'. Its corporate structure is not a metaphor. In the novel, Duncan Sandys, Churchill's son-in-law, coordinates rocket intelligence out of Shell Mex House, the London headquarters of Shell. Shell's Dutch headquarters in The Hague has been commandeered by the Germans as a radio guidance transmitter (simply the tallest building in direct line from firing sites to London, Slothrop is told), but the corporate coincidence feeds his paranoia: British Shell's own people work on liquid propulsion on the other side of the Channel while the Germans transmit guidance signals from their roof. Slothrop, reading a rocket blueprint, notices that the S-Gerät is insulated with a plastic called Imipolex G, developed by 'one L. Jamf for IG Farben' in 1939; the patent is cross-filed with the Swiss Psychochemie AG; ICI has a marketing agreement with the IG frozen at 1939 which, oddly, never seems to have expired. 'For some curious reason,' the narrator observes drily, 'no agreements between ICI and the IG seem to be dated any later than "39".' To Slothrop, the war has disturbed the personnel more than the cartel arrangements.

Shell is, in Slothrop's formulation, the model corporation for a certain postwar shape. 'Who'd know better,' the narrator asks, 'than an outfit like Shell, with no real country, no side in any war, no specific face or heritage: tapping instead out of that global Stratum, most deeply laid, from which all the appearances of corporate ownership really spring?' Pynchon's Rathenau, the industrialist and foreign minister murdered in 1922, becomes a prophet of the cartelised state. Pynchon summons him in a séance in Part I. The dead Rathenau, speaking through the medium Peter Sachsa, lectures on the chemistry of coal-tars. Steel is refined, radiant, ennobled; coal-tar is 'earth's excrement, purged out for the ennoblement of shining steel. Passed over. We thought of this as an industrial process. It was more. We passed over the coal-tars. A thousand different molecules waited in the preterite dung.' Then comes one of the novel's most quoted passages: 'You must ask two questions. First, what is the real nature of synthesis? And then: what is the real nature of control?' The Calvinist vocabulary has literalised itself as industrial chemistry. Preterition is coal-tar. Election is steel. The dyes, drugs, plastics, and explosives of the twentieth century are the passed-over material that industrial synthesis 'redeems', in scare-quotes: the polymer is not a resurrection but a further step in the sorting, and Rathenau is dead when he speaks. The redemption is ironic in the strict sense; it does not undo the sorting so much as make the sorting universal.

The novel doubles Rathenau with Laszlo Jamf, Pökler's old chemistry professor, whose visionary spiel on Kekulé's benzene-ring dream turns industrial chemistry into a political indictment. Kekulé, Jamf says, was granted a vision of the World-Serpent with its tail in its mouth, an emblem of the closed cycle. But 'the meanness, the cynicism with which this dream is to be used'. What corporate chemistry took from Kekulé's benzene ring was not the closed cycle of the cosmos but the closed six-membered ring of aromatic hydrocarbons, the building block of a whole industry. Kekulé's message that 'the World is a closed thing, cyclical, resonant, eternally-returning' is inverted into a system 'whose only aim is to violate the Cycle. Taking and not giving back, demanding that "productivity" and "earnings" keep on increasing with time, the System removing from the rest of the World these vast quantities of energy to keep its own tiny desperate fraction showing a profit.' The System is a thermodynamic pump. It extracts, converts, and disposes; it does not cycle. And it does not survive its own logic: 'The System may or may not understand that it's only buying time. And that time is an artificial resource to begin with... which sooner or later must crash to its death, when its addiction to energy has become more than the rest of the World can supply, dragging with it innocent souls all along the chain of life.' The whole passage is, formally, a chemistry lecture that Pynchon uses to make the thermodynamics of capitalist extraction legible.

Liebig opens two chemistry genealogies in the novel. One runs through Justus von Liebig to August Wilhelm von Hofmann to Herbert Canister to Laszlo Jamf who dreamt Imipolex G: a dye-and-polymer inheritance, the industrial chain that ends in the plastic jacket around the Schwarzgerät. The other, in a different episode and in a different register, goes from Liebig to August Kekulé and by association to James Clerk Maxwell: Liebig is described in this second thread as 'a gate, or sorting-demon' of the kind [Maxwell proposed](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/information-entropy/) in 1867, a hypothetical creature that could reverse entropy by letting fast molecules through a trapdoor while blocking slow ones. From there the same passage draws 'the disturbing intimacy of the Equations with the behavior of the double-integrating circuit in the guidance system of the A4 rocket, the same double-summing of current densities that led architect Etzel Ölsch to design for architect Albert Speer an underground factory at Nordhausen with just that symbolic shape'. Thermodynamic sorting, industrial chemistry, guidance electronics, and imperial architecture share one shape.

Enzian later briefly imagines, in a Pervitin-fuelled trance in Part III, that the Rocket was a decoy and the refinery at Jamf Ölfabriken the 'holy Text'. 'It means this War was never political at all, the politics was all theatre... secretly, it was being dictated instead by the needs of technology... by a conspiracy between human beings and techniques.' The novel immediately supplies its own correction. The younger Schwarzkommando answer Enzian's mystification with blunt materialist fury: 'do you think we'd've had the Rocket if someone, some specific somebody with a name and a penis hadn't wanted to chuck a ton of Amatol 300 miles and blow up a block full of civilians? Go ahead, capitalize the T on technology, deify it if it'll make you feel less responsible.' And the narrator confirms: 'this is stimulant talk here, yes Enzian's been stuffing down Nazi surplus Pervitins these days like popcorn at the movies.'

<hr class="section-break">

Part I has already moved the same sorting logic three centuries backwards, to Mauritius. Pynchon flashes back in its fourteenth chapter to Frans van der Groov, the Dutch ancestor of Katje Borgesius (an agent who will figure in Part II's intelligence games), and thirteen years spent on Mauritius 'systematically killing off the native dodoes for reasons he could not explain'. The passage undoes the [inherited stupid-dodo myth](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/the-dodo-bird-the-real-facts-about-this-icon-of-extinction.html) from the inside. The Dutch colonists see 'stumbling birds ill-made to the point of Satanic intervention, so ugly as to embody argument against a Godly creation'. That interpretation permits the killing. The naming comes first; the muskets follow. The colonial language reduces the animal before it has understood it, and the extinction confirms the reduction retroactively: an adaptation to one ecology becomes proof of unfitness under another. And in the moment when Frans has almost lost heart, the narrator hands him a dream of grace. In a lonely dawn on the Mauritian shore, he witnesses a miracle: the dodoes have been given the Gift of Speech, ranked in thousands on the beach, converted to Christ, tears running down their beaks, sanctified at last. What the colonists' muskets could not justify, their God does with a sermon: the killing is redeemed by conversion, the extinguished species welcomed into the Kingdom of Heaven. Frans, in the end, believes only in the firearm, and the killing continues without the dream.

Right in the middle of this Mauritian scene, the narrator asks: 'But if they were chosen to come to Mauritius,' of Frans's expedition, 'why had they also been chosen to fail, and leave? Is that a choosing, or is it a passing-over? Are they Elect, or are they Preterite, and doomed as dodoes?' The doctrine of predestination has migrated from the Bay Colony pulpit to a seventeenth-century Dutch military venture in the Indian Ocean, and Pynchon lets the transfer stand exposed. What his colonists cannot admit is that the same theology which certifies their own election also certifies the dodo's extinction. The passed-over include, in the same paragraph, the extinct animal and the colonist who exterminated it. William Slothrop's tract, printed by his descendant Thomas Pynchon inside Tyrone Slothrop's ancestry three hundred pages later, is the ethical response the Mauritian settlers were never able to write: holiness for the second Sheep, without whom there'd be no elect.

The [Herero and Nama material](https://www.britannica.com/topic/German-Herero-conflict-of-1904-1907) moves from animal fable to fully historical administrative distance: land turned into map, people into population, resistance into a logistical problem. Enzian is the son of a Russian sailor and a Herero woman; his mother dies on Samuel Maharero's great trek across the Kalahari after the 1904 uprising, drinking water too fast at a waterhole where no one warned the stragglers. The Ovatjimba find him as an infant. Some twenty years later, a German named Weissmann (later Major Weissmann, known as [Blicero](https://gravitys-rainbow.pynchonwiki.com/wiki/index.php?title=Blicero)) arrives in Südwest and takes possession of him (protector and sexual master at once, the relationship inseparable from colonial power), gives him a Rilkean name after the mountain gentian ('Liebchen, this is the other half of the earth. In Germany you would be yellow and blue'), and eventually brings him to Europe. The Zone-Hereros, when they reappear in Part III as the Schwarzkommando, carry that history at the level of grammar. The word they whisper as a mantra, Enzian explains, is *Mba-kayere*. It means 'I am passed over'; in the first instance, death passed over them, and they survived. Enzian himself rejects the 'Will of God Theory' and frames their survival in statistical terms ('our own chances of being right here right now are only a little better than even'), but Pynchon's English gloss carries the Calvinist resonance regardless: 'passed over' is the Latin *praeteritus* made audible.

'Colonies are the outhouses of the European soul,' the narrator writes in the same section of the novel, 'where a fellow can let his pants down and relax, enjoy the smell of his own shit. Where he can fall on his slender prey roaring as loud as he feels like, and guzzle her blood with open joy... Christian Europe was always death, Karl, death and repression.' The claim that European colonial violence prepared, and returned to Europe as, the totalitarian apparatus is not Pynchon's alone. Aimé Césaire, in *[Discourse on Colonialism](https://archive.org/details/discourseoncolon0000cesa)* (1950, trans. Joan Pinkham 1972), described a 'terrific reverse shock' by which Europe accepted from Hitler what it had already licensed in the colonies: 'they tolerated that Nazism before it was inflicted on them...they absolved it, shut their eyes to it, legitimized it, because, until then, it had been applied only to non-European peoples'. Later in the same essay, Césaire names the mechanism directly as 'this boomerang effect of colonization'. Pynchon juxtaposes the Herero and Nama genocide with the Mittelwerk tunnels without asserting a single causal chain of his own, and the juxtaposition is legible as a recurring technology of abstraction: colonial populations turned into logistical problems, bodies processed through files, labour regimes, camps, and orders, the victim converted into a category before being erased.

<hr class="section-break">

The rocket becomes morally clean only if the story stops at engineering. [Mittelwerk](https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/mittelbau-main-camp-in-depth) prevents that. Beneath the Kohnstein massif near Nordhausen lay two parallel tunnels originally built as petroleum reserves for Wifo, the state-owned strategic-reserves enterprise. After the RAF bombed Peenemünde in August 1943, prisoners from what began as a Buchenwald subcamp (redesignated the independent Mittelbau concentration camp in late 1944) converted the tunnels into an underground V-2 production line; Mittelwerk GmbH belonged to Speer's Armaments Ministry, while the SS supplied and controlled the camp labour. The rocket killed before launch. Pynchon lets Slothrop enter the facility in Part III on his own investigation, carrying a forged 'Special Mission V-2' pass, and the strangeness of the tourist underground carries the weight of the scene. The narrator records that liberation, when it came, went first to the light bulbs. 'When the Dora prisoners went on their rampage, the light bulbs in the rocket works were the first to go: before food, before the delights to be looted out of the medical lockers and the hospital pharmacy in Stollen Number 1, these breakable, socketless (in Germany the word for electric socket is also the word for Mother—so, motherless too) images were what the "liberated" had to take.' The bulb, in Pynchon's later tangent on Byron the Bulb, becomes 'one of the great secret ikons of the Humility, the multitudes who are passed over by God and History'. Even the ikon is Calvinist.

The triumphalist spaceflight narrative treats the V-2 as regrettable but necessary prehistory: ugly propulsion on the way to the Moon. V-2 technology did feed postwar missiles and human spaceflight; the same dream of ascent survived the collapse of the Reich with remarkable ease. But the lineage is morally unusable unless the labour remains visible. The rocket's causal arc does not begin at launch. It runs back through the tunnels, the hangings, the production quotas, and the administrative language that converted exhaustion and death into output problems. Franz Pökler, a fictional rocket engineer at Peenemünde, is the System's ideal subject. The narrator traces his knowing blindness: 'with his guilt become a sensual thing, prickling at his eyes and membranes like an allergy, it would seem to Pökler that he could not, even by that day in Weissmann's office, have been ignorant of the truth. That he had known the truth with his senses, but allowed all the evidence to be misfiled where it wouldn't upset him... He had known too, all along, that Ilse was living in a re-education camp.' Pökler is the frighteningly usable man: the competent technical mind, the cause-and-effect engineer, weak enough to be manipulated and decent enough to be damaged by the bargain. The System does not require him to enjoy cruelty. It requires him to accept local frames: this contract, this material, this fairing, this visit, this year.

Ilse, Pökler's daughter, turns that local obedience into an experiment in sparse data. His SS superior Weissmann arranges yearly visits to Zwölfkinder, a children's resort some 280 kilometres from Peenemünde, if the girl is Ilse at all. The interval is too long for knowledge and too precious for rejection. Pynchon writes the analogy directly: 'They have used it to create for him the moving image of a daughter, flashing him only these summertime frames of her, leaving it to him to build the illusion of a single child... what would the time scale matter, a 24th of a second or a year (no more, the engineer thought, than in a wind-tunnel, or an oscillograph whose turning drum you could speed or slow at will...)?' The engineering mind that builds rockets out of discontinuous measurements is asked to build a daughter the same way. The phrase 'Ilse, some Ilse' names the cost of that interpolation. Pökler cannot verify the signal; refusing it would destroy the last structure by which he can still call himself a father. The System has not asked him to believe anything new. It has reused his engineering training. Recognition has been stripped of continuity, and love becomes an input.

Pynchon's advanced IG guidance integrates acceleration over time into velocity and velocity over time into distance. Those who operate the system around Pökler integrate absence over time into obedience. Pökler keeps working partly for Ilse, partly because of Ilse, partly because Ilse has become administratively inseparable from the work. Weber saw religion become a sorting mechanism; Pynchon extends that sorting to the interior life through the same interpolation that turned yearly frames into a moving daughter. In the final Dora scene, Pökler, granted a moment's freedom by the Reich's collapse, wanders into the camp beside the factory whose materials he has worked on for years. The smells, in Pynchon's inventory, are 'shit, death, sweat, sickness, mildew, piss'. He finds a dying woman, sits with her for half an hour, takes off his gold wedding ring, and places it on her thin finger, curling her hand 'to keep it from sliding off'. The engineer, who has spent years inside a system sustained by prisoners' labour, offers a ring he no longer deserves. The gesture is inadequate and knows itself to be inadequate.

> The System integrates absence<br>
> over time into obedience.
> {: .pullquote .pullquote--compact .pullquote--wide}

<hr class="section-break">

Preterition, once it has been named, will not stay in the tract. In the second half of the novel the word colonises everything. Pirate Prentice, who has spent his career processing other people's fantasies for the Firm, decides in Part III that it will be possible 'to die in obscurity, without having helped a soul: without love, despised, never trusted, never vindicated—to stay down among the Preterite, his poor honor lost, impossible to locate or to redeem'. And on the Anubis, the ship of the elect steaming into the Baltic, the narrator distributes the theology across the whole ocean: 'The white Anubis, gone on to salvation. Back here, in her wake, are the preterite, swimming and drowning, mired and afoot, poor passengers at sundown who've lost the way, blundering across one another's flotsam, the scrapings, the dreary junking of memories—all they have to hold to—churning, mixing, rising, falling. Men overboard and our common debris. . . .' And, drily, a page later: 'there is a key, among the wastes of the World... and it won't be found on board the white Anubis because they throw everything of value over the side'.

Pynchon's explicit uses of 'preterite' range from industrial extraction to colonial genocide without making the cases equivalent: coal-tar in Rathenau's séance, Pirate at the point of his private accounting, 'the vast Humility' Enzian invokes, and the Herero survivors who whisper *Mba-kayere*, 'passed over' by the death that took the rest. Ilse is my extension of the word, not Pynchon's: she is present as an object and denied as a continuous subject, administratively used as leverage over her father through yearly frames. What these cases share, once held together, is being passed over as a *subject* even when selected as an *object*: registered as labour, target, resource, stimulus, waste, or error, but never encountered on their own terms. And in the case of the Zone-Hereros the phrase carries a specific double: first, death passed them over in the genocidal campaign begun by [von Trotha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lothar_von_Trotha) against their people in 1904, and then the postwar order passes over them again by forgetting that they exist at all. The word permits Pynchon to name that position without flattening the differences between the systems that produce it.

Two numbered rockets organise the novel's ending: Weissmann's 00000 is fired from the Lüneburg Heath in the last days of the Reich, carrying the Schwarzgerät with Gottfried sealed inside; the Schwarzkommando reassemble the unlaunched 00001 from parts salvaged and traded across the Zone. Enzian kneels to lift an iron tailgate on the truck carrying the disassembled 00001 and thinks: 'Who will believe that in his heart he wants to belong to them out there, the vast Humility sleepless, dying, in pain tonight across the Zone? the preterite he loves, knowing he's always to be a stranger. . . .' Inside the Erdschweinhöhle he is not the only political voice. Josef Ombindi of Hannover leads a faction called the Empty Ones, or Otukungurua, 'Revolutionaries of the Zero', who preach negative birth rate and racial suicide, arguing that the tribe should finish the extermination the Germans began in 1904. The 00001 centres Enzian's hope for continuity, but it also gathers Ombindi's opposing zero-politics into the same project; Enzian reaches a 'strange rapprochement' with him, and the Rocket, in the novel's phrase, contains their argument without resolving it.

But directedness is not freedom. A ballistic system runs on set values: launch, cutoff, angle, target, and, after Brennschluss, no further correction. The colonised return through the coloniser's technology, yet the technology has been engineered inside the same imperial grammar. The question the 00001 poses, and that Ombindi's Otukungurua answer differently, is whether the passed-over can seize the elect's machine and use it to escape the sorting, or whether the machine's logic is such that any use of it repeats what was done to them. Neither the novel nor Enzian settles this; the 00001's actual launch is deferred past the last page. The people who set Feldspath's reference value, who choose what gets stabilised and what diverges, are not neutralised by a Herero assembling his own rocket.

Between Enzian's project and Weissmann's lies the Zone: occupied Germany between May and August 1945, the interregnum between the collapse of the Reich and the recrystallisation of the Cold War at Potsdam, a moment when the sorting apparatus has temporarily lost some of its ordinary coordinates. Squalidozzi, the Argentine anarchist Slothrop briefly meets, articulates the utopian case: 'this War—this incredible War—just for the moment has wiped out the proliferation of little states that's prevailed in Germany for a thousand years. Wiped it clean. Opened it... In the openness of the German Zone, our hope is limitless.' Then, 'as if struck on the forehead': 'So is our danger.' Slothrop, drifting north with Squalidozzi's anarchism still in his ear, arrives at the closest thing to a political programme the novel ever formulates: 'maybe for a little while all the fences are down, one road as good as another, the whole space of the Zone cleared, depolarized, and somewhere inside the waste of it a single set of coordinates from which to proceed, without elect, without preterite, without even nationality to fuck it up'. I read those coordinates as the novel's unreachable good: not a Preterite homeland but the abolition of the division itself, a starting point chosen by a subject rather than a destination imposed on a target.

The Zone does not last. Slothrop's own trajectory through it is the tragedy of its non-permanence. He arrives with all the equipment of a Puritan-descended American paranoid: his sexual encounters, marked on a map of his own making and photographed by Bloat for British intelligence, precede the rocket strikes by several days in the same locations, a correlation that drives the intelligence machinery pursuing him through the Zone. He leaves as a dispersal. Mondaugen's Law, stated in a Peenemünde office, gives the process a formula: 'Personal density is directly proportional to temporal bandwidth.' The narrator extends it: 'The more you dwell in the past and in the future, the thicker your bandwidth, the more solid your persona. But the narrower your sense of Now, the more tenuous you are.' Slothrop, stripped of past and future by a war that has severed him from everything he came from, thins. In one late afternoon he becomes 'a crossroad, after a heavy rain he doesn't recall'; and stands looking up at 'a very thick rainbow here, a stout rainbow cock driven down out of pubic clouds into Earth, green wet valleyed Earth, and his chest fills and he stands crying, not a thing in his head, just feeling natural'. The rainbow belongs to a man who has stopped being an individual and become a place. The narrator's later description is starker: 'It's doubtful if he can ever be "found" again, in the conventional sense of "positively identified and detained." Only feathers... redundant or regenerable organs.' And later still: 'fragments of Slothrop have grown into consistent personae of their own... look for him adrift in the hostile light of the sky, the darkness of the sea. . . .'

The Zone releases Slothrop into formlessness, which is not the same thing as freedom. The preterite do not escape the sorting machine by dissolving; they simply become uncountable. Once the Cold War fences go up in his wake, the road back has been paved over. Squalidozzi's opening is closed. What Slothrop briefly encountered in the rainbow was not a covenant or a destination; the narrator says 'not a thing in his head, just feeling natural.' He does not interpret the arc. I read it as the closest the novel comes to grace: an arc that need not be a coordinate, a guidance path, a mission, or a promise. It comes to a character already thinning towards dispersal, and it means nothing to him.

<hr class="section-break">

Weissmann, in his last days at the northern firing site, has decided to launch a modified V-2, the 00000, with Gottfried inside it. Blicero, in Thanatz's later account, has 'reverted to some ancestral version of himself, screamed at the sky, sat hours in a rigid trance, with his eyes rolled clear up into his head', talking now 'the way the captain in *Wozzeck* sings'. He has 'left 1945, wired his nerves back into the pre-Christian earth we fled across, into the Urstoff of the primitive German, God's poorest and most panicked creature'. His speech to Gottfried the night before the launch is a European's confession. 'America was the edge of the World,' he says. 'A message for Europe, continent-sized, inescapable. Europe had found the site for its Kingdom of Death, that special Death the West had invented. Savages had their waste regions, Kalaharis, lakes so misty they could not see the other side. But Europe had gone deeper—into obsession, addiction, away from all the savage innocences.' Empire, he tells Gottfried, is what happens when a civilisation cannot bear its own edge; it exports the edge, then follows it. 'In Africa, Asia, Amerindia, Oceania, Europe came and established its order of Analysis and Death... Now we are in the last phase. American Death has come to occupy Europe. It has learned empire from its old metropolis... Is the cycle over now, and a new one ready to begin? Will our new Edge, our new Deathkingdom, be the Moon?'

'Fathers are carriers of the virus of Death,' Blicero says, 'and sons are the infected.' And later: 'I want to break out—to leave this cycle of infection and death. I want to be taken in love: so taken that you and I, and death, and life, will be gathered, inseparable, into the radiance of what we would become.' The Rocket, in Blicero's private theology, is a way out of the terrestrial cycle. The ascent is the marriage. Gottfried, dressed in a white lace bridal costume and satin slippers, is sealed inside the Imipolex-lined shroud of the Schwarzgerät. A tiny speaker, implanted separately, transmits Blicero's last words into his ear; there is no return channel. His exact moment of death is unknowable, because the system has no means of registering it. The ritual completes what Weber's iron cage began: a life fully rationalised into an input whose ending cannot even be confirmed.

The rocket ascends. Brennschluss arrives, and the narrator, in the ballistic silence just after cutoff, tips into a passage of racial-imperial cosmology. The burnt-out tail opening swings across the sun; a shadow, a Brocken-spectre, is cast through Gottfried's blond hair. 'What is this death but a whitening,' the narrator asks, 'a carrying of whiteness to ultrawhite, what is it but bleaches, detergents, oxidizers, abrasives—' and then Weissmann's name, itself an etymological joke, unfolds into a genealogy: 'Streckefuss he's been today to the boy's tormented muscles, but more appropriately is he Blicker, Bleicheröde, Bleacher, Blicero, extending, rarefying the Caucasian pallor to an abolition of pigment, of melanin, of spectrum, of separateness from shade to shade, it is so white that CATCH'. In one sentence, Pynchon collapses Blicero's fairytale-oven with the death-camp gas, the town of *Bleicheröde* near Nordhausen (where part of the Peenemünde rocket team had been temporarily evacuated in February 1945) with the racial pallor of the Reich, the industrial oxidisers of Jamf's laboratory with the light passing through Gottfried's hair. The whitening in the ballistic silence just after Brennschluss imagines a system so complete that nothing remains outside it, not even the passed-over.

Blicero's last words, transmitted into Gottfried's implanted speaker, sound almost like grace: 'The edge of evening... the long curve of people all wishing on the first star... Always remember those men and women along the thousands of miles of land and sea. The true moment of shadow is the moment in which you see the point of light in the sky. The single point, and the Shadow that has just gathered you in its sweep...' The elegy has the pity but not the innocence. Blicero is transmitting a beautiful sentence to the boy he is about to kill, into an ear whose voice can never talk back. The rocket, having reached its apex, turns over. Everything after this is fall.

The falling rocket does not land. In the last two pages of *Gravity's Rainbow*, the scene returns to an old Los Angeles cinema on Melrose Avenue whose night manager, Richard M. Zhlubb ('friends and detractors alike think of him as "the Adenoid"'), a scarcely-veiled Richard M. Nixon, has been introduced earlier in the same final episode. The audience is watching a film. The film breaks or the projector bulb burns out; 'It was difficult even for us, old fans who've always been at the movies (haven't we?) to tell which before the darkness swept in.' On the screen there is one last image: 'It may have been a human figure, dreaming of an early evening in each great capital luminous enough to tell him he will never die, coming outside to wish on the first star. But it was not a star, it was falling, a bright angel of death.' Then, formally: 'And it is just here, just at this dark and silent frame, that the pointed tip of the Rocket, falling nearly a mile per second, absolutely and forever without sound, reaches its last unmeasurable gap above the roof of this old theatre, the last delta-t.' The rocket is over us, but has not yet touched. The last delta-t, the infinitesimal interval before impact, is frozen in place forever. The reader has become the audience. The novel places the 00000 over the last cinema in any town, over the reader's chair.

Into this suspended moment Pynchon inserts a hymn attributed to William Slothrop, 'centuries forgotten and out of print, sung to a simple and pleasant air of the period':

> There is a Hand to turn the time,<br>
> Though thy Glass today be run,<br>
> Till the Light that hath brought the Towers low<br>
> Find the last poor Pret'rite one . . .<br>
> Till the Riders sleep by ev'ry road,<br>
> All through our crippl'd Zone,<br>
> With a face on ev'ry mountainside,<br>
> And a Soul in ev'ry stone. . . .

Then, on the novel's last line, the audience is asked to sing along, and the book breaks off: 'Now everybody—'. Gottfried could hear Blicero and the ritual voices, but he could not answer. The novel stops precisely where the audience's answering voice would begin.
