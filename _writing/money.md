<span class="dropcap">W</span>hen I was still living in Cambridge last week, I had to take the train to London every day for work. The lab's PI happened also to live in Cambridge, which is how we came to share the eight o'clock Thursday service. Not daring to strike up conversation on the train, I listened to Audible while covertly observing what she was doing. It looked like a grant application: a mass of figures and names. Inevitable, I thought; September was approaching, old faces leaving and new ones arriving, and grants were an inescapable part of the machinery.

Once we left King's Cross, the PI began to talk. The lab was at UCL, a further twenty minutes' walk from the station, and somewhere along the way the conversation turned to funding. We (strictly speaking 'they', though the intern was permitted to bask in the association) work on Down syndrome and Alzheimer's disease, running population analyses alongside a mouse model. The head of the lab, a professor, had designed a pipeline of over forty mouse lines. Whenever the PI discussed this she became visibly animated; the moment money entered the conversation, we both went quiet. She said that these days only Cambridge and Oxford could afford large-scale experiments; the rest had no budget to speak of. I was surprised and said I had assumed UCL and the other London universities also received government funding. She said they did, but maintaining a single mouse line could cost a thousand pounds a month; add equipment, and government funding fell nowhere near far enough. I asked why Cambridge and Oxford managed. She said because they had money: Trinity College could toss a laboratory some loose change and fund a full-scale mouse experiment, while the rest were left scrambling for grants.

She was right. Inside the ivory tower one becomes immune to such things; everything feels protected, the question of money never arises. But arrive at a working post, find the purse strings tight and no one to shield the work, and the feeling is grim.

Having thought about it at length, I could not blame anyone in particular. I had once read an interview with a Fellow of the Royal Academy in which he said that researchers spend only half their time doing genuine science; the other half is spent lobbying, persuading companies, conglomerates, politicians and officials that one's experiment is brilliant, one's results magnificent, just to claw the funding back. The lifeblood of an entire cohort of researchers sits in the hands of people who have little understanding of research.

Whom to blame, then? The system. Which is why, a few days ago, Verge Science released a video about Jess Phoenix, a volcanologist running for Congress. Her position was compelling: scientists ought to command a louder voice in political circles. As climate breakdown and ecological destruction become politicised, scientists have both the right and the obligation to defend the objectivity of science. Everyone agreed this was a fine idea, but reality disappoints as it tends to. For various reasons, Phoenix did not win the California congressional seat.

The root cause is education. Start from the foundation: if one wants politicians to care about science and respect the voice of scientists, then in a democracy the most direct route is to make more citizens care about science and respect that voice. But this argument slides easily into circularity. One wants the public to receive better education, yet the state may not oblige; the modern apparatus of manufactured ignorance is vastly more sophisticated than its 1970s predecessor. Governments and organisations have learned to deploy media platforms at scale, systematically dismantling any popular pursuit of objectivity. Once even 'we want the truth' has been commercialised and politicised, what use has the public for science?

It is a shrewd arrangement. Over lunch on Tuesday I had the chance to discuss some of this with the lab's professor. We began with the differences between research and other professions (a subject over which my father and I have also argued: he believes I should return home after graduating and serve the motherland; I said fine; he said this is because the motherland raised me, so my achievements should be contributed to the motherland; I said not fine, that science has no borders, and one should not apply the logic of civil engineering to research; he did not agree). The professor said that science did, intuitively, differ markedly from other professions. The difference is especially clear in disease research: a single researcher's results ought, in principle, to be shared with the world; the researcher should be credited, but the achievement should not be offered exclusively to the researcher's nation. Then we moved to fiscal matters, and she said that however one framed it, the present situation was worse than before. With the rise of populism, a significant mass of people had begun to believe that opposing science was the righteous path. Freud sounds impressive; the perpetual-motion machine sounds brilliant; and so one decides these people are talented, and their words pleasant to the ear.

The most direct consequence, as I see it, is this: in a research culture that must fight for every pound of experimental funding, thought quickly becomes monetised. When all eyes fix on whether a project can turn a profit, the intrinsic value and cumulative nature of research struggle to enter the frame. The consequences are severe, particularly in the social sciences. In a previous essay I discussed Amy Cuddy's example. Though Cuddy herself presumably bore no ill intent, the wildfire ignited by her 'power posture' experiment does not become any less damaging on account of her good intentions. The public likes research that is simple and clear-cut: a single gesture, a particular posture, and one's interlocutor perceives one as powerful; how thrilling. But the reverse side: small sample sizes, repeated failures to replicate, the spectre of p-hacking. None of this has dented the PCR-like amplification of Cuddy's results. The question is: when researchers fighting for grants discover that work of this kind can be enormously lucrative, can they still maintain their composure and continue with what is in front of them?

> Thought quickly becomes monetised.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact}

I do not know. I wish the answer were no, but anyone who has ever been pressed for money understands the magnitude of its temptation. Solutions abound: our lab's professor would prefer to keep science and politics separate; Jess Phoenix would prefer to fuse them. As for one ordinary person, all one can apparently do is grumble here.

Money.
