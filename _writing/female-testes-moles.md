<span class="dropcap">T</span>estes are, by definition, male organs. Moles disagree. Females of at least eight mole species carry both ovaries and testes, fused into a single structure called the ovotestis. A study published in *Science* traces this arrangement to gene triplication and chromosomal inversion, two alterations not in the DNA sequence itself but in its three-dimensional architecture. The finding recasts what biologists thought they knew about how sex is determined.

In mammals, sex follows from chromosomes. XY males express SRY, the testis-determining factor on the Y chromosome, which elevates testosterone, suppresses female sex characteristics, and drives male development. XX females, lacking SRY, develop along the female pathway without opposition. Ordinarily, the two programmes do not interfere with one another.

Female moles violate this partition. The testicular portion of the ovotestis does everything a male testis does, bar producing sperm. Females bearing one are more aggressive, more heavily muscled, and carry an external structure resembling a penis. Outside mating season, some have testosterone levels that exceed the male's.

*[Figure: Adult female moles carrying XX chromosomes possess an ovotestis. Credit: Real et al., 2020]*

Stefan Mundlos and colleagues at the Max Planck Institute for Molecular Genetics set out to locate the genetic basis for the ovotestis. Their approach rested on a principle that decades of research have forced into focus: DNA is not merely a sequence of bases. Fitting 1.8 metres of it into a nucleus less than 10 micrometres across requires extensive folding by histones and other proteins, and this folding has consequences. Genes bundled into structural proximity form neighbourhoods called topologically associated domains (TADs); genes within the same TAD interact at far higher frequencies than genes in different TADs, making TADs functional units of gene regulation.

*[Figure: DNA possesses not only a one-dimensional sequence but also a three-dimensional structure. Credit: E.L. Aiden, Knowable Magazine]*

Mundlos hypothesised that the ovotestis might arise not from mutations in gene sequence but from changes in TAD organisation. Using Hi-C (high-throughput chromosome conformation capture), the group analysed both sequence and three-dimensional structure in the Iberian mole (*Talpa occidentalis*), a species whose females carry ovotestes.

The sequences were unremarkable. The architecture was not. Within a single TAD, a gene called CYP17A1 had been triplicated, and the triplication had duplicated its enhancer, a regulatory element whose activation amplifies transcription. Three copies of CYP17A1, each with enhanced transcriptional drive, produce severalfold more of the hydroxylase that catalyses androgen and testosterone synthesis. This alone could account for the ovotestis.

But there was a second alteration. A chromosomal inversion in the regulatory neighbourhood of FGF9 had dragged sequence from an adjacent TAD into FGF9's own, upregulating its expression. FGF9 promotes male sex development directly. In the ovotestis of female Iberian moles, FGF9 expression rivals that in male testes; in mice, by comparison, female ovarian FGF9 sits below a third of the male level.

*[Figure: A chromosomal inversion can 'transport' a regulatory sequence from one TAD into another, thereby redirecting regulation to the latter's genes. Credit: Thomas Splettstoesser; translation: Dinghao Luo]*

Mice carry similar sequences. Mundlos and colleagues engineered the equivalent CYP17A1 and FGF9 alterations in female mice. The edited animals showed elevated testosterone and increased muscle strength. A chromosomal inversion, a simple rearrangement within the genome's architecture, was sufficient to shift sex characteristics.

The adaptive pressure is plain. Moles live by digging: they forage underground and excavate their own dwellings. Males and females alike have powerful forelimbs and reduced hindlimbs, exceptionally strong forearm musculature, and, unlike most mammals, six digits on each forepaw. The testicular portion of the ovotestis secretes testosterone and androgens, both of which promote aggression and build muscle. The Dutch zoologist J. Haeck called the female mole's physique a 'neutral body type', a concession, he argued, to the brutality of subterranean life.

*[Figure: An intersex Northern Cardinal (left) and a wrasse (right). Credit: Shirley Caldwell (left); Aquarium Domain.com (right)]*

These moles are not anomalies so much as reminders. Intersex Northern Cardinals, South American bees, and wrasses inhabit the same continuum; flatworms fence with their penises, each partner trying to pierce the other's skin to fertilise it. As Mundlos put it, 'sex development is an extremely complex process'; intersexuality is 'a manifestation of natural variation'. The mole's ovotestis is one more datum in a growing body of evidence for a simple proposition: evolution does not care about sex.

> Evolution does not care about sex.
