<span class="dropcap">L</span>ooking back, my mental state during last summer had a delicate equilibrium. Quiet living. Finish work, come home, cook, eat, read, sleep, go to concerts alone, go to gigs alone, spend the weekend in my room watching all eight *Star Wars* films. Outside working hours, there was no need for communication. A faint sense of crisis may have existed, but it was not acute. Over time, the quiet even became a kind of requirement.

But I also knew this had not always been the case. There had once been a life full of tension and vitality, a life in which I was not a passive target in social situations, not wounded by my own failures or by other people's casual cruelty. But that tension and vitality had gone. At times the feeling was close to having lost the most basic sense of being alive in the external world: unable to recognise the version of myself I heard described in other people's mouths, unable to determine whether the distortion came from the outside world or from my own numbness to change.

There was a period over the summer when I became acutely sensitive to smells, and would go out of my way to avoid crowded places. My ability to express myself in words began to disappear around then. I only realised how far it had deteriorated later, when recording a programme, discovering that my verbal fluency had regressed to a level I had not experienced in years; many of the things I wanted to articulate could no longer be articulated. It was around the same time that I turned to music, pouring everything related to my inner life into compositions. This was not, of course, a perfect solution. Music cannot convey what language can. Language is impotent at conveying emotion, but in communication it is indispensable. *Homo sapiens* conquered the earth with language, not music; everyone knows this.

Then I discovered that photography and music had become a process of resisting my own existence. To prevent a life stripped of communication from collapsing, one tries everything. My methods at the time were photography and music. I travelled in order to take photographs, mostly with my phone rather than a camera; the aim was to record a state that could not be transmitted. A photographer with two dogs on the coast, the highland mountains: the meaning these things held for me could never be communicated to a second person. This is a postmodern perspective: my viewpoint is mine, and no matter how hard I try, it can only ever be mine. It emphasises a particular *situatedness*, akin to Heidegger's concept of thrownness: we can never walk in another's shoes. So the states I recorded were ultimately not for sharing, but for my own retrospective encounter with past memory. This recording is the individuality of existence.

Music was the other side of this resistance. Music is collectivity. Music makes one overcome fear and stand at the centre of a crowd, declaring the surrender of one's individual self. At a Twenty One Pilots gig in London a while ago, a boy beside me had brought his father along. Before the show started, the boy aimed his phone's flashlight at the far side of the arena; when he saw people over there flash back, he jumped with excitement. Within minutes, a dozen flashlights were pulsing across both sides. The feeling is similar at every gig: an involuntary echo of Rousseau's social contract, trading one's freedom for society's protection. In music the exchange is different: one trades one's individuality for freedom, and receives in return a sense of immersion, a strange satisfaction in the knowledge that 'I do not matter.'

> Music is collectivity.
{: .pullquote .pullquote--compact}

This state continued for a while. Only recently have I begun, consciously, to try again to talk to people who are not yet friends. How long this perhaps futile effort will last, I do not know either.
