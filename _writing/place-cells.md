*This article originally appeared in the 'Neural Musings' column, a series on breakthroughs in neuroscience and the mind. From theology to science, from conjecture to experiment, the pursuit of the mind's holy grail has never stopped. As neuroscience advances, seekers may have glimpsed a parsimonious answer: reductionism. But can reducing mental activity and human behaviour to neural activity bring closure to millennia of inquiry?*

---

'Two roads diverged in a yellow wood, / And sorry I could not travel both,' Robert Frost wrote in 'The Road Not Taken'. How did he choose between them? More fundamentally, how did his brain determine there were two roads in the first place, and predict where each one led? This is not a question for poets alone; every one of us faces analogous choices at every moment. Yet in most cases, we find the right path. How?

Philip Johnson-Laird, the Princeton psychologist, argued in his 1993 book *Human and Machine Thinking* that any entity capable of autonomous movement through an environment requires a representation of that environment. Consider a robot moving across a tabletop. It stores a 'scrolling map' marking obstacles and table edges; as the robot moves, the map scrolls with it, letting the robot 'know' where dangers lie. This scrolling map is the robot's representation of the outside world. Primitive as it is (it cannot, for instance, update in real time to track dynamic changes), it suffices for navigation in a fixed environment: moving from A to B is trivial. For animals, however, navigation is far more complex. An animal must remember where obstacles and dangers are, predict where food and water lie, update its representation in real time as the environment changes, and, in extreme cases, stake its life on the accuracy of this process. Johnson-Laird's point is clear: an internal representation of the external world is indispensable for navigation.

The form such a representation takes, however, remained an open question. Animals might use an 'internal map' analogous to the scrolling map, but this is not the only possibility. Praxic navigation offers an alternative. Imagine driving from home to campus through city streets: under normal circumstances, one needs no map. As long as one can remember the direction to take at each junction, one can combine a sequence of vectors to navigate successfully. This approach is simple, readily repeatable, and far less cognitively expensive than maintaining an elaborate internal map. 'Turn left at the next intersection past the newsstand,' 'the supermarket is at the corner after you exit the school gate': everyone has experience with praxic navigation.

In environments that are relatively stable and rich in external landmarks (tall buildings, shops) that seldom change, praxic navigation is highly effective. [1] But in other circumstances it breaks down. Environments with few external landmarks, for instance, make an internal-map strategy more viable. Based on this reasoning, Edward Tolman, then a psychology professor at the University of California, Berkeley, devised an elegant experiment. [2] He placed rats in a circular arena connected to a single track; a rat that traversed the track and reached the exit received a food reward. Once the rats had learnt the route, Tolman sealed the original exit and attached many similar tracks radiating from the arena. The logic was straightforward: if training had generated an internal map, then when the original route was blocked, the rats should be able to use the map to infer the direction of the reward and run directly toward it. The results confirmed Tolman's prediction: after a brief period of confusion and exploration, rats ran straight to the track pointing toward the food. Tolman concluded that the rats had indeed stored a map in their brains. He called it a 'cognitive map'.

The cognitive map proved to be an enormously consequential concept. As described above, a cognitive map is a detailed internal representation of the external environment. Consider waking in the middle of the night and finding one's way to the bathroom in pitch darkness. This form of navigation, known as 'dead reckoning' or 'path integration', necessarily requires a cognitive map. Without visual input, the brain falls back on its cognitive map, integrating speed, direction, and duration of movement to guide you to the bathroom rather than down the stairs. Countless experiments have demonstrated that animals deprived of multiple sensory modalities can still find hidden rewards in mazes via path integration. [3] Praxic navigation may be important, but the significance of cognitive maps is beyond dispute: in unpredictable environments, having more than one navigational strategy ensures more robust performance.

When Tolman proposed the cognitive map in 1948, no one knew where in the brain it resided. As early as the 1920s, the memory researcher Karl Lashley had run an experiment in which he lesioned different areas of the rat cortex and then tested the animals' ability to learn a maze. He found that navigational ability was inversely proportional to the size of the lesion, regardless of which cortical region was damaged. From this, Lashley formulated his famous 'mass action' theory: the neural activity required for navigation is not concentrated in any one brain region but distributed throughout the cortex. The larger the lesion, the greater the impairment. [4] Over the following decades, this titan's theory dominated neuroscientific thinking about where cognitive maps reside: they were dispersed across the brain, and that was that.

Yet when Lashley lesioned the cortex, it never occurred to him that a function of such importance might lie beneath it. Had he gone just one centimetre deeper, he would not have missed the cognitive map. It took another fifty years before John O'Keefe discovered what Lashley had not: the cognitive map very likely resides in the hippocampus. This subcortical structure, whose layered appearance superficially resembles the cortex, has four distinct subregions: the dentate gyrus, CA1, CA2, and CA3. The dentate gyrus relays incoming information to CA3, which passes it to CA1, which in turn sends it out of the hippocampus (the social-learning functions and circuitry of CA2 remain poorly understood). In 1971, when O'Keefe made his discovery, the functions and circuits of these hippocampal subregions were largely unknown. James Papez had classified the hippocampus as part of the emotional-experience circuit; earlier neuroscientists had considered it a component of the olfactory system. But O'Keefe, recording extracellularly from CA1, found that many neurons fired reliably when a rat occupied a specific location or faced a specific direction, and were essentially silent otherwise. [5] It occurred to him that such a population of neurons could encode every position within a single environment, precisely the condition required for a cognitive map to exist.

In 1976, O'Keefe formally named these neurons 'place cells'. [6] In plain terms, the spatial representation constructed by place cells can support the navigational functions described above. Consider a maze: if, after repeated trials, a rat has formed a cognitive representation of the maze's layout, place cells could 'pave' a cognitive pathway connecting entrance to exit. But place cells blanketing the environment are not sufficient on their own. The brain also needs to know which pairs of cells represent adjacent locations and in what order they are linked; otherwise the system cannot support navigation from entrance to exit. O'Keefe found the cognitive map in the hippocampus, but how do rats actually use it to navigate?

Pursuing this question, O'Keefe turned to the firing patterns of place cells using extracellular electrophysiology. One advantage of this technique is that the recorded signal can be decoded to yield the local field potential (LFP) of the recording site. The LFP reflects the aggregate electrical activity of thousands of cells surrounding the electrode, and this collective activity plays a crucial role in neural information processing. As it happens, hippocampal CA1 produces a distinctive LFP signal: the theta oscillation. Theta refers to waves with a frequency of 6 to 10 Hz (six to ten cycles per second); oscillation refers to the periodic rise and fall of the LFP. Hippocampal theta oscillations appear when rats are moving, and O'Keefe discovered that place cell firing is intimately related to these oscillations. Recording simultaneously from CA1 LFPs and single neurons as rats ran through mazes, he found that CA1 place cells exhibit phase precession. [7]

Imagine walking through a colourful tunnel, its entrance painted red and its exit pale green. As you advance, the place cell encoding your current position fires at a specific 'location' (phase) within the theta cycle. But this cell is not the only one firing during that cycle: cells encoding earlier and later positions also fire, in sequence, before and after the current-position cell. These sequentially firing place cells form what is known as a 'theta sequence'. The sequence does not merely record where you have been and represent where you are; it also predicts where you will be.

Theta sequences are an extraordinarily useful cellular mechanism. With them, rats can not only retrace their steps but also predict, and even evaluate, different routes: which direction leads to a reward? Which path leads home? Which route carries danger? Theta sequences are deeply connected to one of the brain's core functions: prediction. Loren Frank's group at the University of California, San Francisco trained rats on an alternation task: the animals explored a 'maze' with a fork in the middle, choosing left or right at each trial. If the left path yielded the reward on the previous trial, the right path would yield it on the current trial. Using extracellular recordings, Frank's team observed the firing patterns of hippocampal place cells at the choice point. Within adjacent theta cycles, theta sequences alternated between 'playing' the left-path and right-path place cell sequences, as though the hippocampus were rapidly deliberating and weighing the merits of both options. [8]

In the three to four decades since O'Keefe's discovery of place cells, neuroscientists have worked relentlessly to elucidate the mechanisms underlying their generation and function. What many did not anticipate is that hippocampal cells do far more than record location. In 2011, Howard Eichenbaum's laboratory at Boston University discovered 'time cells' in hippocampal CA1. [9] Analogous to place cells, different time cells fire at different time points within a temporal interval, forming a temporally ordered cell sequence. Moreover, researchers have identified 'splitter cells', which fire selectively for one of the two directions at a fork, and 'vector trace cells', which fire when an animal is at a specific relative position to an object and continue firing after the object is removed. Importantly, these cell types do not necessarily map one-to-one onto individual neurons: the same cell can play different 'roles' in different environments, contexts, or tasks. The hippocampus, it is now clear, is a vast cognitive network performing extraordinarily complex computations through cell types that represent key features of the external world.

Do primates, whose cognitive and reasoning abilities differ enormously from those of rodents, possess similar spatial representations? The answer is yes. Recording from individual neurons in the human brain is difficult; typically only epilepsy patients consent to electrode implantation. Yet even these limited data have revealed place cells and phase precession in the human hippocampus, strikingly similar to what is seen in rodents. In a recent paper in *Cell Communication and Signaling*, Joshua Jacobs' laboratory analysed single-neuron activity in the hippocampus of neurological patients navigating a virtual-reality environment, finding that human hippocampal cells encode location and use theta sequences to represent past and future positions along a route. [10] They also found phase precession in other brain regions, such as the amygdala, indicating that cells in these areas encode the temporal relationship of task goals to current experience.

Similar representations of abstract objects have been found in the primate hippocampus. A study published in *Cell* demonstrated that, just as place cells encode space, cells encoding abstract value exist in the monkey hippocampus. These cells encode the 'value' of different images (the magnitude of their associated reward), and the hippocampal activity patterns they produce during image viewing closely resemble those seen in rodents during navigation. [11] This finding suggests that the hippocampus in different species may use analogous representational schemes for whichever cognitive demands are most relevant to that animal.

From Tolman's classic behavioural experiments, through O'Keefe's discovery of place cells, to the latest findings in the primate brain, research on cognitive maps and place cells has come a long way. Yet even nearly a century later, many questions remain. How do place cell representations guide navigation? Why do place cells remain active during sleep? What mechanisms underlie phase precession? What signals does the hippocampus use to construct place cell representations? With optogenetics, large-scale electrophysiology, and two- and three-photon imaging, the answers may not be far off.
