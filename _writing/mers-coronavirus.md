The first case of Middle East Respiratory Syndrome was confirmed in 2012. Between 2012 and 2021, the virus infected roughly 6,000 people and killed 2,000. For a pathogen that has not yet evolved efficient human-to-human transmission, those numbers are devastating. Scientists cannot rule out the possibility that it will one day spread widely between people. There is still no effective vaccine or drug against it, and as the climate warms, the likelihood of an outbreak rises sharply.

Millicent Minayo was not the first person to be kicked by a camel. These seemingly docile mammals can turn remarkably aggressive; one need only try collecting a nasal swab from one. 'It will kick you,' Minayo said. 'It will spit at you. It will urinate on you.'

'Anyone who comes into contact with them could become infected.'

Minayo has spent years studying camels in Marsabit, northern Kenya. These animals, ambling across the desert, carry a nearly invisible danger: the Middle East Respiratory Syndrome coronavirus, MERS-CoV. It belongs to the same family as SARS-CoV-2, the virus behind the COVID-19 pandemic, but its case fatality rate exceeds 30 per cent. According to the World Health Organisation, approximately 35 per cent of confirmed MERS patients have died, against a figure below 3 per cent for SARS-CoV-2 in most countries.

MERS-CoV was first identified in Saudi Arabia in 2012, and within two years it had infected nearly 1,000 people and killed at least 300. The virus does not appear to spread between people without close contact. The most common route of transmission remains camel-to-human, which explains why outbreaks have concentrated in the Middle East.

*[Figure: Countries and territories with confirmed camel-to-camel, camel-to-human, and human-to-human MERS transmission. Credit: WHO]*

Analyses suggest MERS-CoV may stage a comeback, driven in large part by the conditions humanity has created for itself.

Coronaviruses use animals as natural reservoirs. SARS-CoV-2, SARS-CoV, and MERS-CoV have all jumped from animals to humans through various routes. The most probable origin of SARS-CoV-2 is bats, and the same is true of MERS-CoV. A phylogenetic study in the *Journal of Virology* concluded that the viruses most closely related to MERS-CoV come from bats native to Africa, while serological data from camels indicate that these bat-derived viruses had been circulating in camel populations across the Middle East for over twenty years. Around 2010, camels, as intermediate hosts, began transmitting the virus to the people who raise them.

Once inside the human body, MERS-CoV infects epithelial cells lining the bronchi. Because of its similarity to SARS-CoV, immunologists initially assumed it used the same receptor, angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2). When every drug designed to block that binding failed, scientists discovered MERS-CoV exploits a different receptor, dipeptidyl peptidase 4 (DPP4), which is remarkably conserved across bats, camels, and humans, making cross-species infection unsurprising. The human immune response depends heavily on interferons: signalling proteins that prompt neighbouring cells to produce antiviral defences. MERS-CoV efficiently neutralises them, collapsing this first line of defence. Its high fatality rate owes much to this capability, honed across the virus's long evolutionary history inside bats.

A zoonotic virus of this kind requires prolonged contact between humans and its animal host to cross the species barrier. Africa and the Middle East offer ideal conditions. These regions support large, dense camel populations, and human-camel contact is correspondingly extended. Kenya alone is home to more than three million camels; the global total is roughly ten times that figure. In Marsabit, where Minayo works, there are over 224,000, while the official human population is 460,000, barely double the camel count.

Unlike other parts of Kenya, Marsabit has no thriving tourism industry, and 80 per cent of its residents live below the poverty line, deepening local dependence on livestock, especially camels. A camel is hard currency: it can serve as a dowry, or fund a child's schooling. Successive droughts have killed off other livestock, but camels endure. They possess a specialised fluid-regulation system built for desert survival, granting them extraordinary resistance to dehydration; they can survive losing water equivalent to 30 per cent of their body weight. As droughts have worsened, herders have progressively shifted to camels to safeguard their livelihoods.

*[Figure: Animal carcasses on the barren plains of Marsabit in northern Kenya. Many of these plains were once lush grazing lands. Credit: Anthony Irungu]*

Increasingly frequent drought has pushed communities to depend more heavily on camels, greatly increasing exposure. Higher dependence means more people using camels as currency, which means Marsabit's residents must frequently move their animals between villages. These journeys bring the herds into closer contact with wildlife inhabiting the spaces between settlements. 'Disease can spill over from wildlife to camels, and from camels to people,' said Dawn Zimmerman of the Global Health Program. 'Those diseases are always out there in the wild. Given the opportunity, they will cross species boundaries.'

Zimmerman and Minayo are both members of the PREDICT project; Zimmerman leads its Kenya division. Over many years, PREDICT has studied zoonotic diseases in more than thirty countries and identified 1,166 zoonotic viruses, of which only 217 were previously known to infect humans.

When collecting samples, PREDICT scientists must be fully equipped: goggles, masks, full-body protective suits, face shields, rubber boots, and gloves, doing everything possible to minimise infection risk.

Local herders have none of this equipment. 'Camels sneeze too. They cough too,' Minayo said. 'Unlike humans, camels do not wear masks.' The 2018 MERS outbreak in Saudi Arabia alone infected nearly 200 people and killed 56, an alarming toll for a virus that cannot yet transmit efficiently between people.

Although many experts initially believed MERS-CoV would not evolve effective human-to-human transmission in the near term, scientists have since catalogued mutations that may be associated with increased pathogenicity and transmissibility. All the evidence can confirm is that large-scale human-to-human transmission has not yet occurred; the scientific community cannot rule out that it will. 'You never know what the next mutation will bring,' Zimmerman said. 'That is why funding this research is so important.'

*[Figure: Marsabit's dependence on camels grows year by year. Credit: Jacob Kushner]*

Beyond advancing research, educating high-risk populations and providing them with protective equipment is equally vital. Marsabit's residents depend on camel milk: 'Camel milk is everything.' Many locals rely on it for trade. They drink it, use it to make tea, and sell it for income. Yet when milking their camels, they do not wear gloves, and usually have none to wear.

Opportunities for contact extend well beyond milking. To find pasture on increasingly arid plains, herders must trek with their animals across vast distances, sometimes for days without returning home. Each night in the field, a herder sleeps pressed against a camel for warmth. During the day, the herder survives on camel milk alone; boiling equipment is too heavy to carry, so the milk is drunk raw. The deadly risk of MERS transmission travels with them every step of the way.

'We tell them how to protect themselves,' said Dub Wato, Minayo's local colleague. 'Avoid close contact; if contact is unavoidable, wear a mask. After close contact, wash and disinfect your hands; it is no different from fighting COVID-19.' Boiling milk is another crucial precaution: raw milk may harbour any number of zoonotic viruses, and high temperatures inactivate all known varieties.

*[Figure: PREDICT project members collecting a sample from a camel. Credit: Jacob Kushner]*

Scientists are doing what they can to prevent the next outbreak: deploying more physicians and researchers to high-risk areas, sampling camels to detect widespread infection early, educating local populations on prevention. These are necessary measures, but the problem may not be so easily solved. Successive droughts have increased both the frequency and duration of contact between camel-dependent communities and their animals. Drought has also lengthened the journeys herders must undertake in search of pasture. According to a Kenyan government report, 'the frequency of drought has increased to once every one to three years,' and the United Nations projects that Kenya's temperatures will rise by two degrees Celsius by 2050. For the foreseeable future, the time herders spend in close contact with camels will only grow.

And on those long, hungry journeys, the herders still have no choice but to eat the raw flesh of camels that have died of thirst.
