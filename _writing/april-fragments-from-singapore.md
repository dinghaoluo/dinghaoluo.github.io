<span class="dropcap">M</span>aybe this only shows how little I have seen, but this was the first time I had ever spent a full month in a country as a tourist. The tropical weather, the clouds in the morning and the rain in the afternoon, the line of boats moored along the shore: over the course of that month, landscapes that did not belong to home gradually became familiar. Only when I was nearly leaving did the place begin to feel stranger again.

Singapore has a particular cultural climate. Where it is tacky, it is really tacky, while the airport and hospitals can still be polished to a shine. At the centre of the city there is mostly green climbing over tall buildings and hawkers dense with ordinary life; around the city there are several small islands whose names I never quite learned. Hawkers are crowded. The evening I ate at one in Serangoon, I had the good fortune to witness more than a hundred Singaporeans packed into a space of about a hundred square metres, arms folded, standing directly in front of people who were still eating and waiting for their seats. The islands were much better, though once it rained they too became unexpectedly congested.

In the twenty-something days I lived in the city, I moved three times, from Chinatown to Dhoby Ghaut, then from Dhoby Ghaut back to Chinatown. The two places are less than ten minutes apart by car, but their scenes feel entirely different. Chinatown is bright domestic windows and durian in the air. The buildings seem to belong to the shophouse style of the Greater Bay Area, their open ground floors occupied by English breakfast places and Shake Shack, but also by Hainanese chicken rice and Thai milk tea. Dhoby Ghaut sits at the foot of a huge hillside park. The only thing I remember clearly is a university called 'SMU', whose full name I still do not know. One night while staying in Dhoby Ghaut, I suddenly decided to walk down the main road beside the hotel and discovered a large commercial district nearby, rather like the point where Tianhe South meets Tiyu West, except the food was worse and the underground transport less convenient.

I passed over this too quickly above, but the afternoon I found out there were durian stalls in Chinatown, I was ecstatic. The truth is that durian is not cheap, but neither is it impossibly expensive; a box of Musang King was around thirty Singapore dollars. Singapore has prepared many variations on durian, though not, it seems, quite enough of them. It made me miss Nayuki's summer-only Musang King milk tea.

Below the Chinatown hotel were three connected hawkers, one of which had excellent laksa. The first time I ate laksa had been at a high-end restaurant near Russell Square in London. It had neither flavour nor colour, and in photographs looked like sha cha noodles with the saturation turned down. So the hawker downstairs came as a genuine surprise: it turned out this ordinary noodle dish could be so richly fragrant. I ate it faithfully for five days in a row, so that on my last visit before leaving even the owner was startled and exclaimed, 'You eat two bowls a day?'

The last half-week before departure was centred on reading textbooks and watching shows with Shanji. When I occasionally felt refined enough to go outside, I took a few photographs that could only be described as 'uninspired'. By day, Singapore is almost impossible to fault, but at night it is far too bright. The bay at night had the energy of a place recovering from the pandemic. The shore was lined with people taking photographs and eating, forming a dense strip of population between Marina Bay Sands and the nighttime food stalls.

In the days before leaving, I tried several times to begin writing something. I kept feeling that this should be an important node in my life, but the length of my stay in Singapore had diluted the importance of that node, and everything I had written before seemed too fragmented. Next time I come to this city, I hope there will be less anxiety.
