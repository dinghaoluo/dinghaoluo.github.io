I have decided to draft a will.

At first, this was only a vague idea. I mean: whether for the sake of appearing mature or simply because it seemed like a good thing to do, I decided to draft a will. But as experience accumulated, the vague idea took shape, turning into the all-out plan it is now.

I have decided to draft a will. The idea resurfaced recently, sparked by the dim memory of a Weibo post. Two years ago, a blogger live-streamed a suicide attempt, and it caused a minor uproar. Afterwards, another blogger I followed proposed that everyone write their own will. The intention, as far as I recall, was to encourage people to pay closer attention to each other's mental health online. But for me, still in secondary school, it produced a small tremor.

The first time I decided to draft a will, it was on a summer evening with cicadas at the window. I had had my first serious fight with my mother. Life felt like a birdcage, the key hanging from the ceiling, and I did not yet know how to fly. That will was immediate, it was painful, it was a will written towards death for the sake of life. Its entire purpose was to tell a world I had not yet touched about the difficulties and sorrows I had known. Its conclusion was a foregone ending, and for that reason, I did not finish it. What attracted me most about the concept of a will was its contradiction: its meaning is to discuss survival in the face of death, yet the people who need one are precisely those who no longer wish to discuss survival.

But now, this idea seems to have moved somewhere else. The will I want to draft today is not about death, nor is it about living towards death. After the London summer rain arrived, it occurred to me that writing a will is probably just a way to live better. Discussing survival in the face of death: this contradictory act emphasises the emptiness of the latter, and that emptiness is exactly what I do not want. What I need is opportunity, love and ideals, and death only annihilates their opposite; it is living alone that unifies them.

Thinking in this way, a will is still a place for confession, not for abandoning the past but for recording the most honest version of the present. The emptiness of death dissolves the violation of having one's secrets known by others; whether crushes or eccentricities, desires or insecurities, all can stand before the world in their most exposed form, declaring an indelible sovereignty; and the self, long since reduced to ashes, can at last fly to the top of the cage and reach the meaningless key.
