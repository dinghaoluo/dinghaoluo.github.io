<span class="dropcap">K</span>etamine is the active ingredient in anaesthetics routinely used in the operating theatre, and the substance sold on the street as 'Special K'. In March 2019, however, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved a nasal spray called Spravato, whose active ingredient is esketamine (the S-enantiomer of ketamine), as a treatment for depression: the first novel class of antidepressant the FDA had sanctioned in decades. In August 2020, the agency went further, clearing Spravato for the treatment of major depressive disorder with acute suicidal ideation or behaviour, making it the first FDA-approved medication specifically indicated for suicidality. The compound's passage from surgical tool to club drug to psychiatric medication is one of the stranger trajectories in modern pharmacology, and the questions surrounding it remain largely unresolved.

In 1962, Calvin Lee Stevens synthesised a compound designated CI-581 in his laboratory at Wayne State University. Stevens regarded it as a candidate anaesthetic, nothing more. Tests in mice and other animal models confirmed its analgesic properties, and he moved CI-581 into clinical trials. The study recruited twenty inmates from Michigan State Prison; results showed that a dose of one to two milligrams per kilogram of body weight produced robust anaesthesia. Because CI-581 rendered subjects unconscious while simultaneously inducing a sensation of detachment from sensory experience, Edward Domino's wife Toni coined the term 'dissociative anaesthetic'. The compound is now known as ketamine.

One impetus for its development was battlefield medicine. The Vietnam War had begun in November 1955, and field surgery demanded anaesthetics with minimal short-term side effects; ketamine met that requirement. Wounded American soldiers received the drug in growing numbers, and its use spread rapidly to civilian hospitals. In 1970, the FDA approved ketamine for general clinical use, and it became one of the most widely administered anaesthetics in the United States for years to come.

During the counterculture movement of the 1970s, however, ketamine was co-opted for recreation. It became a globally trafficked substance, frequently cut into MDMA. In club scenes across many regions, young people inhaled ketamine after dancing to induce a dissociative, post-exertion euphoria.

The consequences of sustained abuse were severe. Ketamine overdose carries a lethal risk: between 2005 and 2015, nearly one hundred people in England and Wales alone died from ketamine-related causes. Chronic use inflicts a range of catastrophic harms. A review published in *Addiction* noted that animal studies demonstrate ketamine's capacity to produce drug dependence, and clinical data confirm that certain doses induce pleasurable effects with strong addictive potential. Long-term exposure also damages the nervous system. In 1989, John Olney of Washington University School of Medicine reported that rats chronically administered ketamine developed irreversible brain lesions; his results, published in *Science*, gave rise to the term 'Olney's lesions'. Subsequent decades of animal research, from zebrafish to macaques, have consistently shown that ketamine disrupts neurodevelopment and neural repair. In humans, chronic use has been linked to damage of the urinary tract, kidneys, heart, and brain.

In light of these risks, governments worldwide imposed strict controls. The FDA classified ketamine as a Schedule III controlled substance in the 1990s. Few anticipated that the drug would find a second life in the laboratory, let alone emerge as a new hope for the treatment of depression.

In the late 1990s, John Krystal and his colleague Dennis Charney at Yale University administered either 0.5 milligrams per kilogram of ketamine, a sub-anaesthetic dose well below the threshold used in surgery, or a placebo to seven patients with depression via intravenous infusion. Ketamine acts fast as an anaesthetic; its effects typically dissipate within two to three hours. Yet four hours after the infusion, patients began reporting that they felt 'much better'. By the following day, nearly all who had received ketamine described a marked improvement in their depressive symptoms; the placebo group reported no meaningful change. Krystal and Charney tracked the patients for two weeks and found that a single dose produced a sustained alleviation of symptoms. The study was published in *Biological Psychiatry*.

Before this finding, the principal pharmacological treatment for depression was the selective serotonin-reuptake inhibitor, or SSRI: fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft), fluvoxamine (Luvox), and their relatives. SSRIs target the brain's serotonin (5-HT) system. The once-dominant 'monoamine hypothesis' held that insufficient serotonin concentrations weakened emotional regulation and thus constituted the root cause of depression; SSRIs were designed to raise serotonin levels in the synaptic cleft, thereby ameliorating symptoms.

*[Figure: Left: The raphe nuclei, located in the brainstem, project serotonin throughout the brain. Credit: Fvasconcellos, Wikimedia Commons. Graphic adaptation: Dinghao Luo. Right: A serotonergic synapse. SSRIs inhibit the serotonin transporter, raising serotonin levels in the synaptic cleft. Credit: Lundbeck Institute Campus. Graphic adaptation: Dinghao Luo.]*

SSRIs did relieve symptoms in some patients. The theory, however, suffered from two conspicuous weaknesses: SSRIs worked for only a minority of those who took them, and they typically required weeks, sometimes months, before any therapeutic benefit emerged. These shortcomings increasingly undermined the monoamine hypothesis. By the early 2000s, many researchers had abandoned it altogether. The notion that a single neurotransmitter maps neatly onto a single mood state is now regarded as obsolete.

Yet discrediting the monoamine hypothesis offered no new hope to the roughly three hundred million people worldwide living with depression. The field needed a credible theoretical framework to guide both pathological research and future treatment. With Krystal and others pursuing the implications of ketamine, the understanding of depression, stalled for decades, finally began to advance.

In the neural circuitry of emotional regulation, the prefrontal cortex (PFC), a region associated with decision-making, exerts top-down inhibition over the limbic areas involved in emotional processing. This inhibition is mediated primarily by glutamate, the brain's most abundant neurotransmitter. When the PFC's regulatory grip on limbic structures falters, emotional responses can become excessive, potentially giving rise to anxiety, depression, and other psychiatric conditions.

*[Figure: The circuit from the PFC (approximate location within the blue circle) to the limbic areas (approximate location within the red circle) may hold the key to understanding depression. Original image credit: Patrick J. Lynch, Wikimedia Commons. Graphic adaptation: Dinghao Luo.]*

Glutamate acts on two principal receptor types in the human brain: AMPA receptors and NMDA receptors. Recent research has shown that low-dose ketamine enables AMPA receptors to transmit excitatory signals, increasing neuronal activity. Once this heightened activity reaches a sufficient threshold, synaptic plasticity can produce lasting strengthening of the circuit. Bita Moghaddam, a neuroscientist at Oregon Health and Science University, has proposed that sub-anaesthetic ketamine most likely exerts its antidepressant effect by bolstering the PFC-to-limbic pathway, reinforcing the PFC's inhibitory control over the limbic system and thereby restoring the patient's capacity to regulate emotion.

This view aligns with conclusions drawn from animal studies. In a review published in the *Annual Review of Pharmacology and Toxicology*, Scott Thompson and colleagues at the University of Maryland School of Medicine reported that in mouse models of depression, PFC-to-limbic connections had atrophied, accompanied by a weakening of reward-signalling circuits. Low-dose ketamine, they found, 're-awakened' synaptic plasticity in these pathways, rendering the depressed mice more sensitive to reward.

In 2018, Hailan Hu's team at Zhejiang University School of Medicine discovered a distinct mechanism. The NMDA receptor, alongside the AMPA receptor, is the other principal glutamate receptor; one of its most important functions is to permit neurons to engage in high-frequency burst firing. In two papers published simultaneously in *Nature*, Hu's team demonstrated that ketamine suppresses burst firing in neurons of the lateral habenula by blocking NMDA receptors. Because lateral habenula activity is linked to fear, tension, and anxiety, dampening its burst firing directly alleviates depressive symptoms.

*[Figure: Single-cell recordings from the lateral habenula. Compared with the control condition (ACSF, blue), neurons exposed to ketamine (black) lose their capacity for high-frequency burst firing. Credit: Cui et al., 2018, Nature.]*

The picture, however, has grown more complicated. The standard account holds that ketamine itself is the therapeutic agent, acting on NMDA or AMPA receptors in the brain. A 2016 study in *Nature* challenged this premise. Todd Gould's laboratory at the University of Maryland, in collaboration with Carlos Zarate Jr. at the National Institute of Mental Health, demonstrated that (2R,6R)-hydroxynorketamine (HNK), a metabolite produced in the liver as the body breaks down ketamine, was sufficient on its own to produce antidepressant effects in mice. HNK did not block NMDA receptors. It potentiated AMPA receptors. And it did so without inducing the dissociative side effects or the abuse potential associated with the parent compound. If the metabolite, rather than the drug itself, were driving the sustained antidepressant response, then years of mechanistic research had been aimed at the wrong molecule. Zarate's group at NIMH subsequently led a first-in-human Phase 1 trial of HNK. The compound was well tolerated; the antidepressant signal, however, was more equivocal than preclinical data had predicted. The gap between mouse model and human patient, always a liability in psychiatric pharmacology, proved difficult to close.

Even the receptor target remains contested. A 2018 study from Stanford found that pretreatment with naltrexone, an opioid receptor antagonist, blocked ketamine's antidepressant effects, suggesting that ketamine acts in part through the opioid system. If true, the implication would be severe: the 'novel antidepressant' is not so novel after all, but another compound acting on the same ancient receptor family as morphine. A larger follow-up study, also from Stanford, failed to replicate the naltrexone blockade. The question remains open. The field has thus arrived at a peculiar impasse: a drug that reliably alleviates depression within hours, whose mechanism of action, at the level of both molecule and receptor, is still a matter of active dispute.

From this accumulating and in places contradictory evidence, scientists have begun to piece together the mechanism by which ketamine rapidly relieves depressive symptoms. Several aspects remain to be fully elucidated, but existing knowledge has already enabled researchers to begin designing targeted therapeutics.

Depression is an extraordinarily complex illness, and the development of drugs to treat it was never going to be straightforward. Spravato's commercial trajectory reflects both the demand and the difficulty. By 2024, its annual revenue was approaching one billion dollars. In January 2025, the FDA expanded its approval so that Spravato could be used as monotherapy for treatment-resistant depression, rather than only alongside an oral antidepressant. The safety restrictions, however, did not disappear: under the REMS programme, Spravato must still be administered in a certified healthcare setting, under direct observation, with at least two hours of monitoring. It must not be dispensed for home use.

But the clinical landscape surrounding ketamine extends well beyond the FDA-approved nasal spray. Unregulated ketamine clinics have proliferated across the United States, varying wildly in quality; according to an investigation by STAT, the majority failed to follow American Psychiatric Association guidelines with respect to pricing, patient screening, dosing, and frequency of treatment. The problem worsened as telehealth companies began shipping compounded ketamine, nasal sprays, lozenges, sublingual tablets, directly to patients' homes, bypassing in-person evaluation altogether. In October 2023, the FDA issued a safety communication warning that compounded ketamine products had not undergone FDA review for safety or efficacy, and carried risks of respiratory depression, sedation, dissociation, cardiovascular instability, and abuse. The industry had outrun its oversight.

What crystallised the crisis was not a regulatory filing but a death. On 28 October 2023, Matthew Perry was found unresponsive in the hot tub at his Los Angeles home. He was fifty-four years old. The cause of death was determined to be the acute effects of ketamine; contributing factors included drowning, coronary artery disease, and buprenorphine. Perry had been receiving legitimate ketamine infusion therapy for depression, but the ketamine levels in his blood at the time of death far exceeded the range associated with therapeutic use. The investigation that followed exposed a supply chain built on negligence and greed. Five people were charged. Among them were two physicians: Salvador Plasencia, who had texted a colleague, 'I wonder how much this moron will pay', pleaded guilty in July 2025, and was sentenced to thirty months in federal prison that December; and Mark Chavez, who also pleaded guilty. Jasveen Sangha, known in her circle as 'the Ketamine Queen', was charged as the drug's supplier. Perry's personal assistant, Kenneth Iwamasa, who had administered the fatal injection, and a middleman named Erik Fleming were also charged. The case laid bare the consequences of a system in which a Schedule III controlled substance could pass through the hands of licensed physicians operating outside any coherent regulatory structure, driven not by clinical judgement but by the willingness of a patient in pain to pay.

Legislators began to respond. In 2024, Utah enacted the Ketamine Clinics Licensure Act, the first state law to impose specific licensing requirements on ketamine treatment facilities. The DEA proposed new rules to tighten telehealth prescribing of controlled substances as pandemic-era flexibilities expired. These measures remain piecemeal. The ketamine treatment industry has been described, with reason, as a regulatory Wild West.

Steven Meisel, a member of the FDA's advisory committee, is among the sceptics. He has described the party drug as 'dirty', warning that 'its dissociative side effects may not be good news for many patients with depression'. Jeffrey Lieberman, chair of psychiatry at Columbia University Medical Center, has cautioned that over-hyping ketamine as a 'phenomenal drug' while downplaying its side effects, among them dissociative experiences, urinary tract damage, and dependence, will only cause patients unnecessary harm. Meanwhile, numerous companies and laboratories are conducting clinical trials to assess ketamine's reliability for psychiatric conditions beyond depression and suicidal ideation. Krystal and Charney, for instance, have concluded a large-scale trial for PTSD involving nearly two hundred participants.

Given the vast number of people affected by depression, yet also the multiplicity of ketamine's side effects and the inadequacy of current regulatory oversight, the label 'wonder drug' sits uneasily on the compound. As Lieberman has put it, ketamine 'is not pseudoscience that needs to be debunked'; it is 'a drug that needs to be strictly regulated'. One cannot celebrate the promise of a one-hour antidepressant while ignoring the unresolved problems that accompany it. The compound is real. The relief it offers is real. And the harm it can do is real: not only the pharmacological harm of a dissociative anaesthetic administered without adequate safeguards, but the structural harm of a medical system that permits a physician to calculate how much a suffering patient will pay, then supply the drug that kills him. That is the difficulty, and no amount of clinical enthusiasm will resolve it: only regulation, only vigilance, only the slow, merciless accumulation of evidence, and of accountability.
